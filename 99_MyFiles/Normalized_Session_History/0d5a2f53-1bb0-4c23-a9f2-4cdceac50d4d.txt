3. 解决原始 Wiki 思路带来的压力
   如果直接沿用原始的 Wiki 思路，需要读取的 Index 文稿会过于漫长，字数过多。这会导致 Index 文档本身因为类目过于繁杂，变成一个人类无法通过人力去纠错、验证或修改维护的负担，这在现实中是不切实际的。

那么 AI 在进行 Query 阅读时，并不会平白无故地一股脑读完所有的 FrontMatter 和正文，而是会进行逐级阅读：
1. 先去阅读文件夹结构，然后判断 Index。
2. 判断哪些 Index 是需要去读的。
3. 读完 Index 后，找到需要读取的材料，再去读材料的 FrontMatter。

在阅读 FrontMatter 的过程中，如果涉及到了相关字段，那么这篇文稿就一定要读。如果发现涉及到的这些字段跟我要查询的东西几乎没有任何关联，此时 AI 的策略是不再阅读这篇文稿的正文，而是直接把该文稿的 FrontMatter 字段、标题以及位置返还给我。

在输出其他内容的同时，AI 会让我判定一下是否需要阅读这一份文档。对我而言，这并不麻烦，因为通常只有一两份文档需要我人力判定，我可以直接点开手动确认该文档是否是我需要的内容。

1. 后两者无论是处理设计文档还是马匹业务（Business）时，对 Query 的需求都是高精确性、高正确率（High Accuracy）。返回的内容不能出错，且通常需要全面且深入的信息。
2. 而我个人的这个系统，性质更接近于生活层面的 Project Management。它的主要意义不是精准操作，而是帮助我协调日程、处理待办事项并起到督促作用。它承担的是一种“认知负荷转移”的职能。

因此，我对这个系统的 Query 精度要求并不高，可以接受相对粗略的搜索结果。此外，在该系统中：

* 每个 Project 下需要进行的知识库（Knowledge Base）或知识域拆分，其层级和维度也是有限的，不需要无限拆分。

在这之上，如果仍有必要进一步搜索，由于我在 Manifest 的基础上同样维护了 FrontMatter 和文件夹结构，系统可以继续采用分级搜索的方式，去读取 Cluster Manifest 覆盖的具体文件。

这种策略不需要读完所有文件。它会先扫描一圈文件夹结构和文件名，而这些结构大致是由我自己设置的。因此在可预见的未来，它大体上都符合我的认知结构和需求，我不需要实时地去校验它。

Good. Read the Karpathy gist. Here's how I understand the distinction:

**Sparse Indexing (Karpathy's index.md):** A navigation layer. The index points to documents — it tells the LLM *where to look*, not *what's there*. The actual answer still lives in the underlying files. You always need a second read.

**Manifest:** A compression layer. The Manifest *is* a pre-digested answer at the cluster level. For many queries, you never need to read the underlying files at all — the Manifest is the final answer. When it's not enough, you can drill down, but that's optional, not mandatory.

So the distinction isn't about precision level — it's about what the index *is for*. Sparse indexing optimizes for discovery; Manifests optimize for lightweight answer retrieval. That's why they suit different domains: NovelOS and Horses require exact retrieval (sparse index → document body), while LifeOS needs approximate awareness (Manifest → done). 2. **Token economy as a first-class design constraint** — every architectural choice here (hierarchical folder, FrontMatter filter, Manifest) maps directly to a token cost. This could be made explicit: the design *is* an optimization over token usage at query time.

3. **The folder structure as a shared mental model** — it's not just navigation; it's a communication artifact between you and the AI. When you look at `02_Knowledge/01_World/01_Natural`, that structure encodes your ontology of the project. The AI learns your categories.

4. **Humans as schema designers, not data entry clerks** — the role shift is from "I maintain the wiki" to "I design how the wiki organizes itself." That's a meaningful reframe of what AI assistance looks like in practice.

5. **Graceful degradation** — unlike RAG, this system degrades predictably. If the index is slightly stale, you get slightly stale results; you don't get hallucinated vectors.

**1. Tacit Knowledge Gap（隐性知识鸿沟）**

这是最经典的框架，源自 Michael Polanyi 的名言："We know more than we can tell." 他提出的 **Polanyi's Paradox** 后来被 MIT 经济学家 David Autor 专门用来讨论自动化的边界——很多工作之所以难以自动化，恰恰是因为执行者自己也说不清自己在做什么。你描述的现象是这个悖论在 AI 部署场景中的精确复现。

例子：一个资深编辑在审稿时，会"凭感觉"判断一篇文章的节奏是否对——段落之间的轻重缓急、哪里该留白、哪里该加速。如果你让她写一份 style guide 给 AI，她写出来的规则可能只覆盖了自己实际判断依据的三分之一。剩下的那些她"就是知道"但写不出来。

再比如，一位销售主管在判断一条线索值不值得跟进时，依赖的不只是公司规模和预算这些显性指标，还有邮件里的措辞语气、对方回复的节奏等等。他自己做判断毫无问题，但你让他把这些写成 prompt 给 AI 做 lead scoring，他根本不知道从何说起。

**2. The Articulation Bottleneck（表达瓶颈）**

这个说法把重心放在"瓶颈"上——AI 的能力不是瓶颈，流程设计不是瓶颈，人把自己脑子里的东西"倒出来"的能力才是。它比 tacit knowledge 更具行动指向性，暗示了一个工程问题：如何疏通这个瓶颈。

例子：一个团队想用 AI 自动生成周报。每个人其实都知道自己老板想看什么——哪些事要重点提、哪些一笔带过、坏消息怎么包装。但当你让他们把这些"写作策略"变成 instructions 的时候，大家写出来的都是"请总结本周工作"这种空洞的指令。真正让周报"好用"的那些判断，全卡在了从脑到嘴的那一步。

再比如，一位设计师审视 UI 时，会立刻感知到某个界面"不对劲"，但你问她哪里不对，她可能需要很长时间才能组织出语言——"间距不匀称"只是她最终找到的一个近似描述，真正触发她不适感的可能是一整套视觉节奏的失衡，而这套东西她没有词汇去讲。

**3. The Specification Gap（规格缺口）**

这个概念偏工程和产品视角。它强调的是：在人与 AI 系统之间，存在一个结构性的"规格缺口"——人的真实需求和人能写出来的 specification 之间，天然有一段距离。这个词在软件工程里有传统（requirements engineering 中"用户说不清需求"是个老问题），但放在 AI agent 的语境下获得了新的紧迫性，因为 agent 对 specification 的依赖远比传统软件更重。

例子：一家公司想让 AI agent 处理客服工单的分级。产品经理写了一套清晰的分级规则：P0 是系统宕机、P1 是功能故障、P2 是体验问题。但实际运行中发现 AI 的分级和人工分级总是对不上。原因是，老练的客服在分级时还会考虑"这个客户上周刚投诉过""这个问题虽然小但影响的是付费功能"——这些从来没有被写进规则，因为写规则的人觉得它们"不算规则"，只是"常识"。

再比如你举的知识库例子——推行者在 spec 里写的是"每位员工维护个人决策知识库"，但 spec 里不可能写"请考虑员工会因为担心上级查看而自我审查"。这个顾虑不在任何人的需求文档里，但它真实地决定了产品的成败。

**4. The Legibility Problem（可读化困境）**

这个概念借自 James C. Scott 在 *Seeing Like a State* 中的框架。Scott 说的是，国家为了管理社会，需要把复杂的现实"变得可读"（legible）——简化、量化、标准化。但这个过程必然会丢失大量 local knowledge。AI 部署面临的是同一个困境：要让 AI 接手工作，你就必须把工作"变得可读"，写成 prompt、写成 workflow、写成 knowledge base。但真正让工作运转的那些东西，往往恰恰是抗拒被可读化的。

例子：一家律所想让 AI 辅助合同审查。律师可以把审查要点列成 checklist 给 AI，但资深律师在实际审查中依赖的一个关键能力是"嗅到风险"——比如某个条款的措辞虽然在法律上没问题，但根据这个行业的潜规则，这种写法通常意味着对方在后面某个环节会给你挖坑。这种判断根植于多年的行业浸泡，它本质上就是 illegible 的。

再比如，一个社区运营想用 AI 来辅助内容审核。她可以写出明确的规则：禁止人身攻击、禁止广告。但她日常真正在做的判断远比这微妙——某条评论字面上没有违规，但结合发帖人的历史行为和当前社区氛围，她知道这条评论会引发争吵。这些判断根本没办法被"翻译"成 AI 能执行的规则。

Horsys manages an equine import business. Invoices, contracts, veterinary records, regulatory filings, marketing assets, and — less obviously — my accumulated judgments about people: who negotiates honestly, who doesn't, which veterinarian I trust with which kind of horse. NovelOS supports a long-running collaborative science fiction and game design project — hundreds of world-building documents, character profiles, plot mechanics, and a category I came to call Pending Terms: contradictions and unresolved design decisions that I deliberately keep alive rather than forcing to resolution. LifeOS coordinates everything else — my teaching, my writing, my academic research, my job search, my daily schedule — across half a dozen professional roles that share a single calendar and a single person.

Each system follows the same core pattern. At the entry point is what I call the Dump: a folder where anything can be dropped without pre-sorting. Raw documents, voice notes, screenshots, half-formed thoughts. An orchestrator reads each item, classifies it, and routes it to a specialized agent — one handles finance, another archival, another scheduling. An Archive Agent writes the processed knowledge into a structured, markdown-based knowledge base, generating metadata fields and maintaining a tag system that links related concepts across files.

The retrieval logic is where my architecture diverges most sharply from Karpathy's original pattern. His sparse index — a single file listing everything in the knowledge base — works well at moderate scale. But my novel project alone contains several hundred interconnected documents. A single index becomes unreadable, for the machine and for the human who needs to verify it. So I designed a hierarchical search: the system first reads the folder structure itself — which encodes my own categorical thinking about the domain — then reads the relevant index files, then scans the metadata of candidate documents, and only then reads the full text of the files it has identified as relevant. At each stage, irrelevant material is filtered out, reducing both cost and noise.

For LifeOS, where I need approximate awareness rather than surgical precision, I replaced the sparse index entirely with what I call Manifests — compressed, AI-generated summaries of each knowledge cluster. For most queries, the Manifest is the answer. The system never opens the underlying files.

Every one of these design choices was shaped by a single constraint: cost. The first night I cold-started NovelOS — feeding it twenty-odd documents, the shortest a few hundred words, the longest several thousand — the system burned through roughly a hundred dollars in API fees. That is not a sustainable architecture. Over months of iteration, I reduced the per-query cost by a factor of roughly a thousand. My monthly spend across all three systems is now in the low tens of dollars. That reduction did not come from better hardware or cheaper models. It came from architectural discipline — from designing every retrieval path to read only what it must.

## The Articulation Bottleneck

The architecture worked. Retrieval was fast. Cost was manageable. And then I hit a problem that no amount of engineering could solve.

When I designed the metadata schema — the structured fields that the AI uses to filter and classify each document — I sat down and listed every category I thought I would need. The AI suggested additions. Between us, we produced something that looked comprehensive. It was not.

In use, I discovered two things. First, the AI had generated fields that were technically plausible but practically useless — categories I would never actually search by. Second, and more troubling, it had missed fields that turned out to be essential. I couldn't have told it what was missing, because I didn't know myself. I only discovered what I needed when I searched for something specific and failed to find it — and then asked: *why didn't the system surface this?* The answer, each time, was that the knowledge existed but had been filed without the one category that would have made it findable. A category I hadn't known to create, because I hadn't known I thought in those terms.

The philosopher Michael Polanyi called this "tacit knowledge" — the things we know but cannot articulate. *We know more than we can tell.* But in practice, the problem has a second face that Polanyi's formulation doesn't quite capture: we also need more than we can describe.

A small example. I built an email assistant as part of LifeOS and instructed it to fetch my messages every hour. What I actually needed — what I could not have specified at the time — was to not be overwhelmed at the start of a new day. These are not the same instruction. A system that takes me literally satisfies the specification and fails the person.

I began calling this gap *Human Sloppiness* — not as a deficiency, but as a design constraint. Human knowledge is contradictory, contextual, emotionally weighted, and constantly evolving. Human needs are often legible only in retrospect. Any architecture that demands perfect specification up front will fail — not because the technology is inadequate, but because the human cannot provide what the system requires.

So I designed around it. The Pending Terms folder in NovelOS holds contradictions I cannot yet resolve — naming decisions, mechanical conflicts, settings that could develop in two incompatible directions. Most knowledge systems demand resolution. Mine allows ambiguity to persist. And that ambiguity turned out to be generative: contradictions revisited months later, with new context, often split into two productive paths rather than collapsing into one forced answer. The human checkpoints in the retrieval chain — moments where the system returns a document and says *I'm not confident this is relevant; you decide* — exist because I learned that a system which never asks for help is a system that silently gets things wrong.

None of this is optimization in the engineering sense. It is accommodation — architecture shaped not around what the machine can do, but around what the human cannot cleanly express.

People ask me why a historian is building AI systems. It is the wrong question. The right question is why so few humanists are.

My academic training is in the history of technology — specifically, the micro-history of everyday technologies in late Qing and early Republican China. Flush toilets. Gas stoves. Electric grain mills. Not the grand inventions that make it into textbooks, but the mundane objects that quietly restructured how people lived, thought, and related to each other. What this discipline teaches you is not how technologies work, but what they do to the people who use them — and how wide the gap can be between the two.

That gap is precisely what I encountered building these systems. The engineering problem — retrieval, indexing, cost optimization — is solvable. Engineers solve it daily. The unsolved problem is the one I described in the previous section: the articulation bottleneck, the Human Sloppiness, the irreducible distance between what a person knows and what they can specify. A humanist doesn't solve this problem either. But a humanist is trained to see it — to expect it, to design for it rather than around it.

I tested this intuition against a concrete thought experiment. If my partner — a highly trained business executive, analytically formidable — were to build the exact same knowledge system from identical requirements, our end products would likely be roughly equivalent. She would be faster. I might be more precise in certain structural choices. But the output, the cost, the time — roughly the same.

So the difference is not in the product. It is in what the process of building it does to the person who builds it.

She would walk away with a tool. I walk away having been forced to externalize my own cognitive structure — my contradictions, my judgment patterns, the things I knew but had never made explicit. The system I build is a mirror, not just a machine. And that process of self-articulation changes me, regardless of whether the output is measurably superior.

This is not a claim of superiority. It is a claim about what we are actually doing when we design these systems — and what we risk losing if we treat the entire process as purely technical. Most AI discourse asks: *is the system better?* I am asking a different question: *what happened to the person who built it?*

2. **Tacit knowledge exclusion** — The system captures what was written down. It misses what people *know but never wrote*: judgment patterns, relationship histories, contextual instincts.

**Argumentative job:** Set up the need for a multi-tier architecture.

### III. Three Tiers (~600–700 words)

**The core proposal.** An organization should maintain three distinct knowledge layers:

**Tier 1: Personal Knowledge Base**
- Owned by the individual employee
- Captures their working habits, judgment patterns, relationship notes, decision heuristics
- Designed around Human Sloppiness (from Article 1): the system learns how this person actually works, not how the org chart says they should
- Not surveillance — this is the employee's own cognitive infrastructure, like a personal notebook that gets smarter
- The architecture from Article 1 (Dump → agents → structured KB) applies here at individual scale

**Tier 2: Initiative / Project Knowledge Base**
- Shared among a team working on a specific initiative
- Core function: **information alignment** — ensuring that what each team member knows, doesn't know, and *incorrectly believes* is made visible
- File-system based, structured markdown, same architectural pattern
- Each member can pull from and contribute to the shared KB
- Lifespan is bounded: when the initiative ends, the KB is sealed

**Tier 3: Company-Wide RAG**
- Traditional vector-embedded system for the full organization
- But with a critical operational change: **updated only at project boundaries** (start and end), not continuously
- When an initiative KB is sealed, its contents undergo a final update and are embedded into the company RAG
- This dramatically reduces maintenance cost and RAG drift

**Diagram suggestion:** Three concentric layers with data flow arrows. Personal feeds into Initiative feeds into Company. Company provides initial context back to new Initiatives.

### IV. Information Alignment (~400 words)

This is not a communication failure. It is a structural one: there is no mechanism for making the *shape* of each person's understanding visible to others.

The Initiative KB provides that mechanism. When a team member contributes to the shared knowledge base, discrepancies surface naturally — not through meetings or status updates, but through the structure of the knowledge itself.

This is not a failure of the architecture. It is a design constraint that the architecture must accommodate. Any serious enterprise deployment needs to account for:
- Regulatory boundaries (GDPR, sector-specific rules)
- Data classification (what can the AI see vs. what it cannot)
- The hybrid workflow: AI-assisted in some domains, human-only in others, running side by side

### III. The PIE Stack: Three Layers Every Organization Needs (~700 words)

**The core proposal.** Named, structured, concrete.

**Layer 1: Personal Knowledge Base**

Owned by the individual. Captures their working habits, judgment patterns, relationship notes, decision heuristics. Not surveillance — the employee's own cognitive infrastructure, like a notebook that accumulates structure over time.

Designed around what I've called *Human Sloppiness* (reference Article 1 lightly): the system learns how this person actually works, not how the org chart says they should. It accommodates contradiction, incomplete specification, and the gap between what someone says they need and what they actually need.

The architecture: a zero-friction ingestion point (the Dump), agent-based routing, structured markdown knowledge base with AI-generated metadata. The same pattern I run personally, applied at individual employee scale.

**Layer 2: Initiative Knowledge Base**

Shared among a team working on a specific project or initiative. File-system based, structured, bounded in lifespan — it lives as long as the initiative does.

Core function: **information alignment.** Not "sharing knowledge" in the soft, aspirational sense. Making the *shape* of each person's understanding visible to others — including the gaps, the contradictions, and the things they incorrectly believe to be true.

This matters because teams routinely operate on misaligned information without knowing it. Different members hold different — sometimes contradictory — beliefs about the same project. There is no meeting, no status update, no Slack channel that reliably surfaces this. The Initiative KB does, structurally, because discrepancies become visible when two people contribute knowledge about the same domain and it doesn't match.

A harder truth: in many organizations, people actively protect undocumented knowledge because it is the source of their perceived value. The Initiative KB doesn't solve that political problem. But it makes the knowledge gap detectable — which is the precondition for addressing it.

**Layer 3: Company-Wide RAG**

The traditional vector-embedded system. But with a critical operational change: **updated only at initiative boundaries** — when a project starts (initial context pull) and when it ends (final knowledge embed). Not continuously.

When an Initiative KB is sealed at project completion, its contents undergo a final structuring pass and are embedded into the company RAG. This dramatically reduces maintenance cost, eliminates RAG drift during active projects, and ensures that what enters the company-wide system has already been validated through use.

**One-sentence compliance note:** In one of my own deployments — supporting executive operations under European regulatory constraints — the full system was designed and prototyped, but every actual operation remains human-performed. The architecture must accommodate its own boundaries.

**Diagram suggestion:** Three concentric layers. Personal (innermost) → Initiative (middle) → Enterprise RAG (outermost). Arrows: Personal feeds into Initiative. Initiative feeds into Enterprise at project close. Enterprise provides initial context back to new Initiatives at project start.

**Argumentative job:** This is the structural proposal. It should feel both novel and obvious.

**I can draft this.** Will need your review on how much technical detail to include from Article 1.

### IV. Information Alignment Is Not Knowledge Sharing (~400 words)

**Content:** The most underreported problem in enterprise AI.

Distinguish sharply between three things:
- **Information** — what is true and known
- **Misinformation** — what is believed to be true but isn't
- **Disinformation** — what is deliberately misrepresented

Most enterprise knowledge systems address only the first. They assume the problem is access — "if only people could find the right document." But the actual failure mode is the second: team members holding incorrect beliefs about the project, the client, the timeline, the constraints, without anyone — including themselves — knowing it.

The Initiative KB surfaces this structurally. When two people contribute knowledge about the same domain and it doesn't cohere, the gap is visible in the knowledge base itself — not in a meeting where one person speaks louder.

**Brief reference forward:** "The dynamics of information alignment within organizations — and the distinction between what teams don't know, what they incorrectly believe, and what they deliberately obscure — deserve their own treatment, and will receive it in a forthcoming piece."

**Argumentative job:** Elevate the Initiative KB from "shared folder" to "structural alignment mechanism." This is the insight executives haven't encountered.

**I can draft the structure.** This section carries a claim that needs to feel grounded, not theoretical — I may ask you for a concrete example from your experience.

### V. The Real Cost Conversation (~400 words)

**Content:** Two cost dimensions executives need to understand.

**1. Architecture-level optimization is the most a company can do.**

Token economy, context management, retrieval path design — these are engineering decisions that compound across every employee, every query, every day. The difference between a well-architected and poorly-architected system is not incremental — it can be orders of magnitude. (Reference Article 1: several-thousand-fold reduction in per-query cost through architectural discipline alone.)

**2. Employee behavior is the part you can't fully control — and shouldn't try to.**

Training workshops on "how to prompt efficiently" have uncertain ROI. The cost-benefit analysis of that training — does the saved token cost justify the workshop cost? How long do the habits persist? — is itself a waste of executive bandwidth. The better strategy: build the optimization into the architecture so that efficient behavior is the default, not the aspiration.

**3. De-blackboxing.**

A well-structured system has fewer opaque AI reasoning steps. When something goes wrong, the failure point is identifiable — in which layer, at which retrieval stage, in whose knowledge contribution. This matters for debugging, for auditing, for compliance, and for the most important thing of all: trust. People trust systems they can inspect.

**Argumentative job:** Translate the token economy insight from Article 1 into executive language — operational cost, ROI, risk management, auditability.

### VI. Close (~250 words)

**Content:** Return to the opening.

The woman who knows who to call at 2 AM — her knowledge isn't in the RAG system because the RAG system was never designed to hold it. It was designed to hold documents. She is not a document.

The PIE Stack doesn't extract her knowledge against her will. It creates a structure where her knowledge has a place to live — in her own Personal KB, in her team's Initiative KB, and eventually, when a project closes, in the organization's collective memory. At each layer, the knowledge is shaped by the person who holds it, not flattened into a vector.

The question executives need to ask is not "how do we get more value from our AI investment." It is prior to that:

*Does your organization know itself well enough to teach itself to a machine?*

If the answer is no — and for most organizations, it is — then the first investment is not in better AI. It is in better self-knowledge. In structures that make the undocumented visible, the misaligned detectable, and the human irreplaceable.

**Emotional job:** Quiet conviction. Not a sales pitch — a reframe that stays.
