**V. The Difference Between a Knowledge Base and a Cognitive Map**
~450 words

**An information layer** that doesn't just store documents but actively routes relevant information to the people approaching a decision point — before they know they need it.

**A state layer** that maintains a living registry of what the organization is doing — every initiative, its owner, its status, its last sign of life — so that zombie projects (still consuming budget, no longer active) and orphan decisions (made but never recorded) become structurally impossible to hide.

The third layer is what turns a knowledge base into cognitive infrastructure. Without it, you have a better filing cabinet. With it, you have a system that can tell you, twenty-four hours before a board meeting, that two of the participants have never seen the data underlying the main agenda item — and can brief them in advance, so the meeting starts at the decision rather than at the catch-up.

**Section II — Three Failures Wearing One Mask (~380 words) — Draft**

The alignment failure in that meeting was not one problem. It was three, layered on top of each other — and the stack is worth examining carefully, because each layer has a different character and a different cost.

The first is information alignment. The relevant document existed. It was sent. It was received by five of the eight people in the room. The failure was not in the information itself but in its distribution — a gap between the people who held it and the people who needed it, invisible to both. This is the layer most organizations recognize, and the one they have tried, with varying success, to address through search systems, shared drives, and knowledge repositories. The results are familiar: vast collections of documents that are technically accessible and practically unfindable.

The second layer is progress alignment. In any organization running dozens of parallel initiatives, no single point maintains a live picture of current status. Projects stall while the organization continues treating them as active. Projects complete quietly while resources — budget, attention, headcount — keep flowing toward them. Progress information is generated continuously but captured only episodically, at review cycles almost always too infrequent and too formal to reflect what is actually happening on the ground.

The third layer is the one that almost never gets named. Call it ignorance alignment: I don't know that you don't know something, and you don't know that I know it. Before any substantive cross-departmental conversation can begin, there is an invisible preliminary negotiation — each party mapping the other's knowledge boundaries, testing what can be assumed, calibrating what needs to be explained. Nobody announces this process. It consumes anywhere from ten minutes to the full duration of the meeting, and it takes the time that was supposed to go to decisions.

This third layer carries a harder edge than the others. Some of what organizations don't share is not simply undocumented — it is strategically undocumented. Relationship histories, judgment patterns, knowledge of which clients require which handling: these live in individuals partly because individuals have learned, correctly, that exclusive knowledge is organizational leverage. The solution to ignorance alignment is not a software deployment. It requires, first, acknowledging that the problem exists — and that much of what drives it is rational behavior operating inside a structure that rewards information asymmetry.

**Section III — The Alignment Tax (~210 words) — Draft**

McKinsey's survey of more than a thousand senior executives found that nearly 40 percent of their working time goes to internal meetings. Bain's analysis of those meetings found that in a typical large company, roughly 40 percent of meeting time is devoted not to decisions but to informing — synchronizing context, re-establishing shared ground, catching participants up on developments they missed. The arithmetic is straightforward: close to one working day in five, for every senior leader in the organization, is consumed by alignment work before any substantive discussion can begin.

This is not inherently unreasonable. Alignment at the top of a large organization is genuinely high-value work — imperfect information shared among intelligent people, correctly, costs less than decisions made on mismatched foundations. The problem is the fraction of that time spent rebuilding context that already exists: re-explaining backgrounds that were already explained, re-confirming facts that were already confirmed, re-establishing shared ground that had formed last week and since evaporated.

That fraction is not the cost of complexity. It is the cost of running a complex organization without infrastructure for its own cognition.

**核心思路：把"承认无知"从一件公开的、有代价的事，变成一件私密的、零代价的事。**

Ignorance alignment 之所以最难解决，不是因为人们不愿意分享，而是因为承认"我不知道X"在职场里有真实的代价。你不能要求人们更勇敢——这是道德说教，而且无效。你要做的是改变结构，让承认无知不再需要勇气。

What happens when a historian builds an AI knowledge system

𝗦𝗖𝗜𝗦𝗦𝗢𝗥𝗦

The first time I tried to fit living knowledge into a retrievable shape, I used scissors.

I was writing a novel — a dozen cities, three years of war, roughly twelve characters whose lives collide at unpredictable intervals. I don't outline. I build each character entirely in my mind, watch them move, and let the story emerge from their collisions. But when I sat down to write, the timelines contradicted each other. Lives I had imagined in full turned out to be incompatible the moment they shared the same world.

So I wrote out each character's story from beginning to end — dense, emotionally saturated documents, tens of thousands of words each — and then transferred everything into a spreadsheet. One row per event, one column per character. Cold, clinical. I printed it out, took a pair of scissors, and cut each row into a strip.

I moved the dining table onto the balcony to make room. I spread every strip across the living room floor and began rearranging them by hand, tearing the name cell off the leftmost column and pasting it onto another strip, trying to find an order in which all these people could exist simultaneously. When I was finished, I had twenty-some pages of clean, uncontradicted timeline — and a large pile of torn fragments. Moments I had lived inside vividly, but that could not survive contact with structure.

That was years before LLMs entered my life. I have encountered versions of this problem again and again since then, and they were never just mine. Most recently, it surfaced when Andrej Karpathy published a short document on GitHub.

𝗧𝗛𝗘 𝗕𝗟𝗨𝗘𝗣𝗥𝗜𝗡𝗧

Three days before I began writing this, Karpathy — founding researcher at OpenAI, former head of AI at Tesla — posted what he called an "LLM Wiki." The premise was disarmingly simple: instead of vector databases and embedding pipelines, you build a knowledge layer from plain Markdown files organized in folders, with a single index file serving as a navigational map. The LLM reads the index first, then decides what to read next. He framed it as a design pattern, not a deployment manual. Within days, developers were replicating it. In the comments, a smaller group said something more interesting: they had already been running systems like this, independently, for months.

I was one of them, though I had arrived from a very different direction. I am a historian by training — not of wars or states, but of the everyday technologies that quietly restructure how people live. How flush toilets reshaped domestic architecture. How gas stoves changed not just cooking but the rhythm of a household's day. How electric lighting rewrote labor law long before legislators understood what had happened. My professional reflex, when I encounter any technology, is not to ask what it can do but what it does to the person using it. That reflex led me to the same architectural pattern Karpathy described — not through machine learning research, but through the problem of making my own knowledge navigable without destroying what made it alive.

𝗧𝗛𝗥𝗘𝗘 𝗦𝗬𝗦𝗧𝗘𝗠𝗦, 𝗢𝗡𝗘 𝗔𝗥𝗖𝗛𝗜𝗧𝗘𝗖𝗧𝗨𝗥𝗘

Today, I run three separate knowledge systems, all built on the same structural pattern.

Horsys manages our equine import business: invoices, contracts, veterinary records, regulatory filings — and, less formally, my accumulated judgments about the people we work with. Who negotiates honestly? Which veterinarian I trust with which kind of condition. NovelOS supports a long-running collaborative science fiction and game design project — hundreds of world-building documents, and a category I came to call Pending Terms: contradictions I deliberately keep alive rather than forcing to resolution. LifeOS coordinates everything else — teaching, writing, research, job search — across half a dozen professional roles that share a single calendar and a single person.

Each system follows the same core pattern. At the entry point is the Dump: a folder where anything can be dropped without pre-sorting — raw documents, voice notes, screenshots, half-formed thoughts. An orchestrator classifies each item and routes it to a specialized agent. An Archive Agent writes the processed knowledge into a markdown-based knowledge base, generating metadata and linking related concepts across files.

Where my architecture diverges from Karpathy's is in retrieval. His sparse index — a single file listing everything — works at a moderate scale, but my novel project alone contains several hundred interconnected documents. So I designed a hierarchical search: the system reads the folder structure first, which encodes my own categorical thinking, then the relevant index files, then the metadata of candidate documents, and only then the full text. At each stage, irrelevant material falls away. For LifeOS, I replaced the index entirely with Manifests — compressed, AI-generated summaries of each knowledge cluster. For most queries, the Manifest is the answer. The system never opens the underlying files.

Every one of these choices was shaped by cost. The first night I cold-started NovelOS, the system burned through roughly $100 in API fees. Over months of iteration, I brought my monthly spend across all three systems into the low tens of dollars — not from better models, but from a single principle: design every retrieval path to read only what it must.

𝗧𝗛𝗘 𝗔𝗥𝗧𝗜𝗖𝗨𝗟𝗔𝗧𝗜𝗢𝗡 𝗕𝗢𝗧𝗧𝗟𝗘𝗡𝗘𝗖𝗞

The architecture worked. Then I hit a problem that no amount of engineering could solve.

When I built the metadata schema, I listed every category I thought I would need. The AI suggested additions. Between us, we produced something that looked comprehensive. It was not. The AI had generated fields that were technically plausible but practically useless. Worse, it had missed fields that turned out to be essential — and I couldn't have told it what was missing, because I didn't know myself. I only discovered what I needed when I searched for something and failed, and then asked: why didn't the system surface this? Each time, the knowledge existed but had been filed without the one category that would have made it findable. A category I hadn't known to create, because I hadn't known I thought in those terms.

Michael Polanyi called this tacit knowledge — we know more than we can tell. But his formulation misses a second face of the same problem: we also need more than we can describe. I built an email assistant and told it to fetch my messages every hour. What I actually needed was not to be overwhelmed at the start of a new day. A system that takes the first instruction literally satisfies the specification while failing the person who wrote it.

The things we know but cannot articulate, the things we need but cannot describe, the ways our requirements contradict themselves over time — I came to see all of these as faces of a single constraint, and I began calling it 𝗛𝘂𝗺𝗮𝗻 𝗦𝗹𝗼𝗽𝗽𝗶𝗻𝗲𝘀𝘀. Not a deficiency. A design condition. Human knowledge is contradictory and contextual. Human needs are legible only in retrospect. Any architecture that demands perfect specification will fail, because humans cannot provide what the system requires. The question is not how to overcome this sloppiness. It is how to respect it.

So I designed around it. Pending Terms holds contradictions I cannot yet resolve. Most knowledge systems demand resolution; mine allows ambiguity to persist, and that ambiguity has proven generative — contradictions revisited months later often split into two productive paths rather than collapsing into one forced answer. The human checkpoints I built into the retrieval chain exist because a system that never asks for help silently gets things wrong.

None of this is optimization in the engineering sense. It is accommodation — architecture shaped around what humans cannot cleanly express. And the process of building it changed me in ways I did not expect. Every folder encoded a judgment about how my own knowledge is structured. Every metadata field I kept or discarded reflected a priority I had never articulated. Every unresolved contradiction was an acknowledgment that premature closure would cost me more than ambiguity.

Most writing about AI asks whether the system is better. Building these systems forced me to ask a different question: what happened to me — to my self-knowledge, my awareness of my own patterns — in the process?

And the systems also showed me, with uncomfortable clarity, where that self-knowledge stops.

𝗪𝗔𝗟𝗞𝗜𝗡𝗚 𝗜𝗡𝗧𝗢 𝗧𝗛𝗘 𝗕𝗔𝗥𝗡

Our company has been switching stables — the facility where imported horses are housed, trained, and cared for between transactions. Over two weeks, we visited four operations. All credible. At this level of the market, any barn on our shortlist has already cleared the basic thresholds: acreage, infrastructure, credentials, and competitive record. On paper, the differences are marginal.

I walked into one of those four barns and knew it was right. Not after the tour. Not after comparing notes with my team. During the first walk through the property, before anyone had made a pitch.

I could not, at that moment, have told you why. It was only weeks later — while maintaining my knowledge base and working through the decision with my staff and business partner — that the reasons began to surface, one by one, as if they had been waiting to be named.

The flies. Or rather, the absence of them. The barn had lizards along the fence line and almost no mosquitoes. The fly count was remarkably low for a facility housing that many horses. What this meant, I realized afterward, was that the property's ecosystem had been deliberately managed — the barn owner had designed the environment to be robust against pests, not just treated the symptoms. At the time, I did not consciously think that there were very few flies here. I did not notice a ladybug and drew a conclusion. But the accumulated absence registered somewhere below conscious attention, in the part of the mind that reads an environment before the analytical mind catches up.

The horses themselves. How they stood in their stalls — calm, unbothered by strangers walking through. This is not nothing. A horse that startles when unfamiliar people enter its space is telling you something about how it is handled on the days when no visitors come.

The way the barn owner talked about individual animals. Every owner we met was in sales mode — that is the nature of these meetings, and I expect it. But within that register, there were differences that no pitch could disguise. One owner, asked about a particular horse, answered immediately: its name, its quirks, the small behavioral details that make it a specific animal and not a line item. Another owner paused — just slightly, just long enough — and you could feel a mental spreadsheet being consulted. Both could produce the information. The difference was where it lived in them.

And then there were things so fine-grained that I hesitate to describe them, because in isolation they sound almost absurd. One barn owner, describing a horse jumping a fence, mentioned the way the animal's neck extended forward on landing — a small thrust, a minor imbalance that most observers would never register. It matters because a rider feels it: that fraction of a second where the balance shifts, where the horse's center of gravity moves slightly ahead of where the rider expects it. No buyer would say, "I didn't like this horse because of the forward neck motion on landing." They would say "something felt off" or simply that the horse wasn't for them. But a barn owner who notices this — who carries this level of physical attention toward the animals in their care — is a barn owner who will catch the things that never appear on an evaluation form. A barn owner who doesn't notice will let those things persist, invisible and consequential, until they eventually cost a sale or, worse, an injury.

None of this was a checklist I carried into those visits. None of it was a scoring rubric applied after the fact. It was the accumulated effect of years spent in this industry and adjacent ones — knowledge of animal husbandry, of Southern business culture, of ecological systems, of how people perform trustworthiness versus how they practice it. All of it operates as a single integrated act of perception, most of it below the threshold of language.

I want to be precise about what this is, because the easy word — intuition — is the wrong one. Intuition implies something approximate, closer to luck than to knowledge. What I am describing is the opposite. It is the most precise thing I do. It is the full weight of everything I have learned, every partnership I have navigated, every failure I have watched unfold — operating not as a sequence of criteria but as a unified whole. It activates when the situation demands it, and it is reliable precisely because it cannot be decomposed. Separate the parts, and each one becomes trivial. Together, they constitute something no schema could reproduce.

This is what I mean by 𝗛𝘂𝗺𝗮𝗻 𝗘𝗳𝗳𝗼𝗿𝘁𝘀. Not a soft phrase. A precise one. It names the total capacity — judgment, experience, knowledge, moral reasoning, all of it integrated beyond the reach of conscious retrieval — that a person brings to bear in the moments that matter. Most of this capacity cannot be articulated, even by the person who carries it. That is not its weakness. That is the condition of its power.

You cannot teach this to an AI. The most sophisticated self-improving knowledge system can only approach it asymptotically — learning more about how this particular person operates, what patterns their decisions follow, what conditions activate their deepest expertise. A good system can support this capacity. It cannot become it.

If Human Efforts name something AI cannot replicate, then the prevailing narrative about replacement is pointed in the wrong direction.

## Takeaways — 我认为这篇文章必须讲明白的事

**1. 组织不是有效率问题,是有认知对齐问题。** 而对齐问题有三层,这三层几乎从没有被作为一个统一的失败命名过。

**2. 第三层(ignorance alignment)是最贵也最难的,而难度不是技术的,是结构的。** 在大型组织里,当众承认"我不知道 X"对个人的职业轨迹有真实的代价。人们隐藏自己的无知,不是怯懦,是理性行为。你不能靠呼吁勇气解决它。你必须改变结构,把"承认无知"从一件公开的、有代价的事,变成一件私密的、零代价的事。

**3. 必须把两件事小心地分开。** 一种是"我不知道 X,但如果知道了就能更好地做这次决定"——这类知识是可以在人之间流动的,而这是系统应该处理的。另一种是人身上不可剥离的那部分——判断力、关系经验、对客户的直觉——那不是"组织恰好还没掌握的信息",那是人的 Human Efforts。系统不应该试图提取它,不是因为技术做不到,而是因为它不属于组织。这条线必须清清楚楚地画出来。

**4. 主流解法是错的——把一切堆进一个公司级 RAG/知识库/搜索系统。** 这是 Karpathy 模式放大到组织层,而且会失败,原因不是技术不够好,而是它把三类完全不同的知识当作同一种东西处理。对个人而言,这种架构感觉像监控;对组织而言,它得到的是人们防御性输入的、失真的数据。比没有基础设施更糟。

**5. 正确的解法是三层架构,三种归属。** Corporate KB(公司所有)、Initiatives Bank(项目内共享,边界明确)、Personal Knowledge Base(个人所有,法律和基础设施双重排他)。三层各有生命周期、各有访问规则、各有归属。会议前的私密简报——也就是 ignorance alignment 的真正解法——发生在系统跨三层读取(尊重边界)之后,为每个参会者生成只属于他自己的 briefing。这个"meeting prep"不是人与人之间协调出来的,是系统内部自我协调、加上有边界的提取,然后交给每个人的。

**6. 归属边界不是一个 feature,是前提。** 没有这条边界,人们不会信任系统,会防御性输入,数据会被污染,基于污染数据的洞见比没有洞见更危险。归属边界同时是解决 ignorance alignment 的技术前提(承认无知的私密性靠 PKB 的排他性成立),也是在 AI 时代重新定义雇佣关系的起点——员工离职时带走自己的 PKB,公司留下的是真正属于公司的东西。这是一次诚实的账。

**7. 这篇文章的结尾应该回到开头那个会议,但让读者看到那 20 分钟"补课"其实本可以发生在会议前 24 小时,在每个人自己的私密简报里。** 会议可以从它本来应该开始的地方开始。这是一个具体的、可感的、能被执行层立即想象出来的改变。

## Section II — Three Failures Wearing One Mask (~510 words) — Draft

The alignment failure in that meeting was not one problem. It was three, stacked on top of each other, and each layer has a different character and a different cost.

The first is information alignment. The relevant document existed. It was sent. It was received by five of the eight people in the room. The failure was not in the information itself but in its distribution — a gap between the people who held it and the people who needed it, invisible to both. This is the layer most organizations recognize, and the one they have tried to address through search systems, shared drives, and enterprise knowledge bases. The results are familiar: vast collections of documents that are technically accessible and practically unfindable.

The second layer is progress alignment. In any organization running dozens of parallel initiatives, no single place maintains a live picture of current status. Projects stall while the organization continues treating them as active. Projects finish quietly while budget and attention keep flowing toward them. Progress information is generated continuously but captured only episodically, at review cycles too infrequent to reflect what is actually happening on the ground.

The third layer is the one that almost never gets named. Call it ignorance alignment: the gap between what a group needs to share in order to work at depth, and what each person actually arrives with. Not expertise. Not judgment. The baseline — the common floor of facts, definitions, procedural details, and situational context that every substantive conversation assumes is in place before it begins.

Here is what this looks like in practice. Last week I ran a one-hour technical session for roughly two hundred colleagues, showing how to stand up a custom email agent from scratch. The session had been carefully designed — a detailed initial prompt setting scope and parameters, a structured path for refining the agent through live dialogue. When it worked, it worked beautifully. Out of the full hour, the part where I was actually demonstrating the core idea took about twelve minutes.

The remaining forty-eight minutes went to questions that had nothing to do with the idea itself. Where do I install this? Can you send the access link? Which folder does this file go in? How do I configure the connector? Every one of these should have been resolved before the session began, by someone other than me, through some channel other than the demonstration itself.

This is the shape of ignorance alignment. Not a failure of skill, but a failure of baseline. Two hundred capable professionals in the room, each missing a different small piece of context — not from lack of preparation, but because no part of the organization was responsible for making sure everyone arrived with the same floor under their feet.

And what makes this layer expensive is what keeps it invisible. Most of the people in that room who could not install Claude Code did not raise their hands. Asking a baseline question in front of senior colleagues — when everyone else appears to follow — is a professional risk that people correctly calculate and mostly decline to take. Half the ignorance in that room was never surfaced. The twelve minutes of substance reached only the portion of the audience who had already, somehow, found their way to the floor.

## Section II — Three Failures Wearing One Mask (~580 words)

The alignment failure in that meeting was not one problem. It was three, stacked on top of each other, and each layer has a different character and a different cost.

The first is information alignment. The relevant document existed. It was sent. It was received by five of the eight people in the room. The failure was not in the information itself but in its distribution — a gap between the people who held it and the people who needed it, invisible to both. This is the layer most organizations recognize and have tried to address through search systems, shared drives, and enterprise knowledge bases, with familiar results: vast collections of documents technically accessible and practically unfindable. The residual cost then lands on individuals — typically the assistants and operations staff closest to the flow, who learn to compose every email as if for an archive, to push files proactively to anyone who might conceivably need them, and to send "just in case" copies they know most recipients will never open. The missing infrastructure becomes a quiet, continuous tax paid by the people least equipped to refuse it.

The second layer is progress alignment. In any organization running dozens of parallel initiatives, no single place maintains a live picture of current status. Projects stall while the organization treats them as active; projects finish quietly while budget and attention keep flowing toward them. Progress information is generated continuously but captured only episodically, at review cycles too infrequent to reflect what is actually happening on the ground.

The third layer is the one that almost never gets named. Call it ignorance alignment: the gap between what a group needs to share in order to work at depth, and what each person actually arrives with. Not expertise. Not judgment. The baseline — the common floor of facts, definitions, procedural details, and situational context that every substantive conversation assumes is in place before it begins.

Here is what this looks like in practice. Last week I ran a one-hour technical session for roughly two hundred colleagues, demonstrating how to build a custom email agent from scratch. The session had been carefully designed — a detailed initial prompt setting scope and parameters, a structured path for refining the agent in real time. Of the full hour, the part where I was actually demonstrating the core idea took about twelve minutes.

The remaining forty-eight minutes went to questions that had nothing to do with the idea itself. Where do I install this? Can you send the access link? Which folder does this file go in? How do I configure the connector? Every one of these should have been resolved before the session began, by someone other than me, through some channel other than the demonstration itself.

This is the shape of ignorance alignment. Not a failure of skill, but a failure of baseline. Two hundred capable professionals in the room, each missing a different small piece of context — not from lack of preparation, but because no part of the organization was responsible for making sure everyone arrived with the same floor under their feet.

And what makes this layer expensive is what keeps it invisible. Most of those who could not install Claude Code did not raise their hands. Asking a baseline question in front of senior colleagues — when everyone else seems to follow — is a professional risk people correctly calculate and mostly decline to take. Half the ignorance in that room was never surfaced. The twelve minutes of substance reached only the fraction of the audience who had already, somehow, found their way to the floor.

## Section III — The Alignment Tax (~210 words, 之前已确认，无改动)

McKinsey's survey of more than a thousand senior executives found that nearly 40 percent of their working time goes to internal meetings. Bain's analysis of those meetings found that in a typical large company, roughly 40 percent of meeting time is devoted not to decisions but to informing — synchronizing context, re-establishing shared ground, catching participants up on developments they missed. The arithmetic is straightforward: close to one working day in five, for every senior leader in the organization, is consumed by alignment work before any substantive discussion can begin.

This is not inherently unreasonable. Alignment at the top of a large organization is genuinely high-value work — imperfect information shared among intelligent people, correctly, costs less than decisions made on mismatched foundations. The problem is the fraction of that time spent rebuilding context that already exists: re-explaining backgrounds that were already explained, re-confirming facts that were already confirmed, re-establishing shared ground that had formed last week and since evaporated.

That fraction is not the cost of complexity. It is the cost of running a complex organization without infrastructure for its own cognition.

## Section IV — The Question Behind the Question (~280 words) — New Draft

What, exactly, is this alignment tax paying for?

My instinct, as a historian of everyday technologies, is not to ask what a new tool can do. It is to ask what the tool does to the people working around it. Every technology reshapes the person who lives inside it, usually before anyone notices, and usually in ways the inventors did not plan for. The interesting question about enterprise AI is not which tasks it can absorb but which parts of a working life it will quietly protect — and which parts it will quietly hollow out.

The first is to build a single enterprise-wide knowledge base and hope the problem dissolves into a sufficiently capable search function. The second is to rely on a small number of unusually experienced managers whose personal judgment, memory, and relationships are expected to cover the gaps the system cannot reach. Both responses fail for the same reason. They treat three fundamentally different kinds of knowledge — the organization's institutional memory, the shared state of its current work, and the irreducible capacity each individual brings to their role — as if they were one thing. And then they hope that either better technology or better people will resolve the question of boundaries on their own.

The question of boundaries does not resolve on its own. It has to be built. And what needs to be built is not one knowledge system, but three — arranged in a specific relationship to each other, with a specific commitment about what belongs to whom.

## Section IV — The Question Behind the Question (~340 words) — Revised

As a historian of everyday things, my reflex with any new technology is not to ask what it can do but what it does to the people living around it. The sewing machine did not just make clothing cheaper; it redrew the boundary between home and wage work, and it did so decades before anyone noticed. The wristwatch did not just tell time more accurately than a pocket watch; it changed how workers and supervisors negotiated the length of a shift. Tools reshape the people who use them, usually before anyone calls it a change.

That reflex matters here because the question enterprise AI must ultimately answer is not only about what tasks the tool can perform. It is also about the people working around it — not just how the tool is supposed to function, but how a person is meant to use it, and what the shape of that use does to them over time.

Most organizations working on the alignment problem today are relying on some combination of two responses. One is architectural: feed everything into a single company-wide knowledge base, embed it into a vector database, and trust that better retrieval will close the gaps. The other is human: lean on a handful of unusually experienced managers whose memory, judgment, and relationships are expected to carry the organization across the spaces the system cannot reach.

Neither response is wrong on its own terms. The difficulty is that three quite different kinds of knowledge end up being treated as one. There is information that can move between people — decisions, policies, project history, the procedural baselines that every substantive meeting assumes. There is the shared, living state of current work — what is active, what has stalled, what has quietly finished. And there is the integrated capacity each professional carries inside them: accumulated judgment, relationship memory, the instinct for when a particular client needs a phone call rather than an email. I call this last category **Human Efforts** — the total capacity a person brings to the moments that matter, most of it developed over years and most of it impossible to articulate, even by the person who carries it. It is not organizational information that happens to live in one head. It is human capacity, developed by an individual, belonging to that individual. A system that tries to extract it will not just fail; it will damage the thing it was trying to reach.

What the problem needs is an architecture built around the distinction. Three layers. Three kinds of ownership. A clear, enforceable commitment about what flows between them and what does not.

## 重构后的 Section IV (~280 词)

As a historian of everyday things, I tend to distrust accounts of technology that describe only what the tool can do. The interesting question is always what the tool does to the people working around it — and that question is usually answered years after the tool is adopted, by people who never saw it coming.

The last time organizations faced a knowledge-distribution problem at this scale, the answer was the vertical filing cabinet. When the Library Bureau began promoting it in the 1890s, it was sold as a space-saving device. What it actually did was transfer institutional memory out of the heads of senior clerks and into a physical system that any employee could query. Companies discovered, for the first time, that they could reassign a client relationship without a ritual handover, or dismiss a veteran without losing the business he carried. An efficiency product turned out to be an epistemic one. The historian JoAnne Yates called the shift that followed "control through communication" — a phrase worth remembering, because the tool we are now deploying will be asked to do something of the same order.

The dominant responses to the current alignment problem do not rise to that order. Some organizations are funneling everything into a single company-wide knowledge base and hoping retrieval will close the gaps. Others are leaning on a handful of unusually experienced managers to carry the organization across the spaces the system cannot reach. Neither response is wrong, exactly. Both are incomplete in the same way: they flatten categories of knowledge that are not the same kind of thing, and then ask one mechanism to hold all of them.

What the three layers actually need is three layers.

## Section V — Three Rings (~580 words) — Draft

What those three layers are is a set of ownership commitments, and the ownership is what makes the rest of it work.

At the outermost ring sits the Corporate Knowledge Base. This is the organization's institutional memory: archived projects, policy decisions, filed reports, closed initiatives. It belongs to the company. When a new employee joins, the portions of this archive relevant to their role are surfaced into their personal space on day one. Each active project — a product launch, a due diligence, a regulatory filing — has its own bounded knowledge space, shared among the people working on it and sealed at the project's end. This is where the living state of current work actually exists: what is active, what has stalled, what the team has learned in the last two weeks. Its shape is bounded by the initiative, and when the work is done, the bank is closed, structured, and handed back to the Corporate layer.

At the innermost ring sits the Personal Knowledge Base. This space belongs, exclusively and permanently, to the individual. It holds what a person actually carries with them through their working life: their private judgments about the people they work with, their notes on relationships and clients, the instincts they have developed over years and cannot fully articulate. What I call **Human Efforts** — the integrated capacity a professional brings to the moments that matter, most of it developed over years and most of it impossible to articulate even by the person who carries it — lives in this layer and only in this layer. No organizational agent reads it. No enterprise system indexes it. Information flows in freely; it flows outward only when the person chooses to submit something upward, into an initiative or a report.

Now return to the Tech Demo. Imagine running it again tomorrow, with this architecture already in place. Twenty-four hours before the session, the system — drawing from past tech rollouts in the Corporate KB, the relevant Initiative Bank, and the participant list — assembles a private briefing for each of the two hundred people expected to attend. Nothing shared with their manager, nothing visible to their colleagues. Just a quiet note delivered into their own Personal Shell: *based on tomorrow's session, you may want these three things.* The install instructions. The connector configuration their team uses. A working example from the adjacent division. The next day, the questions that consumed forty-eight minutes the last time simply do not come up — not because people are smarter, but because no one had to reveal, in public, what they did not know.

This is not a technology miracle, and anyone who has actually built such a system knows better than to present it as one. The architecture respects Human Sloppiness — the gap between what people can articulate and what they actually know — but it does not resolve it. People will skim their briefings. Files will be ingested into Personal KBs the person never reads, leaving the system convinced they know things they do not. We honor Human Efforts, and we still need effort from humans — the pun is intentional. What the architecture does is clear the space around the effort, so the effort can go toward the part of the work only a human can do.

One commitment makes the whole structure trustworthy. The Personal KB belongs to the person. It travels with them when they leave. No company mechanism can extract it against their will. This is not a feature to negotiate; it is the condition under which the other two layers become safe to use. I suspect that in the decade ahead, the question of what belongs to the employee and what belongs to the company will be among the more consequential unsettled questions in the employment relationship. # The Triple Alignment Problem

*working title — alternatives below*

---

Every important cross-departmental meeting contains a sequence that no one names.

The meeting is scheduled for an hour. Eight people, three from regional offices. The agenda has four items; the last is the one that matters — a resource allocation decision that will shape the next two quarters.

Twenty minutes in, someone from finance mentions a number. It is not new. It appeared in an email three weeks ago, sent to a distribution list that included five of the eight people present. The other three — including the two regional leads whose input is the reason the meeting exists — never received it.

The meeting pauses. Someone shares the attachment. The finance lead explains context. A clarifying question leads to a second, then a third. Twenty minutes pass. The conversation is useful, but it is not the conversation anyone came to have.

The meeting ends. The allocation decision is deferred. No one describes what happened as a failure, because it felt like a normal meeting. It was a normal meeting. That is the problem.

What occurred in that room was not a scheduling error or a communication lapse. It was a structural failure in how the organization distributes knowledge. It happens every day, in every large company, and it does not have a name.

---

The alignment failure in that meeting was not one problem. It was three, stacked on top of each other, and each layer has a different character and a different cost.

The first is information alignment. The relevant document existed. It was sent. It was received by five of the eight people in the room. The failure was not in the information itself but in its distribution — a gap between the people who held it and the people who needed it, invisible to both. This is the layer most organizations recognize and have tried to address through search systems, shared drives, and enterprise knowledge bases, with familiar results: vast collections of documents technically accessible and practically unfindable. The residual cost then lands on individuals — typically the assistants and operations staff closest to the flow, who compose every email as if for an archive, push files proactively to anyone who might need them, and send "just in case" copies they know most recipients will never open. The missing infrastructure becomes a quiet, continuous tax paid by the people least equipped to refuse it.

The second layer is progress alignment. In any organization running dozens of parallel initiatives, no single place maintains a live picture of current status. Projects stall while the organization treats them as active; projects finish quietly while budget and attention keep flowing toward them. Progress information is generated continuously but captured episodically, at review cycles too infrequent to reflect what is actually happening on the ground.

The third layer is the one that almost never gets named. Call it ignorance alignment: the gap between what a group needs to share in order to work at depth, and what each person actually arrives with. Not expertise. Not judgment. The baseline — the common floor of facts, definitions, procedural details, and situational context that every substantive conversation assumes is in place before it begins.

Here is what this looks like in practice. Last week I ran a one-hour technical session for roughly two hundred colleagues, demonstrating how to build a custom email agent from scratch. The session had been carefully designed — a detailed initial prompt, a structured path for refining the agent in real time. Of the full hour, the part where I was actually demonstrating the core idea took about twelve minutes.

The remaining forty-eight minutes went to questions that had nothing to do with the idea itself. Where do I install this? Can you send the access link? Which folder does this file go in? How do I configure the connector? Every one of these should have been resolved before the session began, by someone other than me, through some channel other than the demonstration itself.

This is the shape of ignorance alignment. Not a failure of skill but a failure of baseline. Two hundred capable professionals, each missing a different small piece of context — not from lack of preparation, but because no part of the organization owned the job of making sure they arrived with the same floor under their feet.

And what makes this layer expensive is what keeps it invisible. Most of those who could not install Claude Code did not raise their hands. Asking a baseline question in front of senior colleagues — when everyone else appears to follow — is a professional risk people correctly calculate and decline to take. Half the ignorance in that room was never surfaced. The twelve minutes of substance reached only the fraction of the audience who had already, somehow, found their way to the floor.

---

McKinsey's survey of more than a thousand senior executives found that nearly 40 percent of their working time goes to internal meetings. Bain's analysis of those meetings found that in a typical large company, roughly 40 percent of meeting time is devoted not to decisions but to informing — synchronizing context, re-establishing shared ground, catching participants up on developments they missed. The arithmetic is straightforward: close to one working day in five, for every senior leader in the organization, is consumed by alignment work before any substantive discussion can begin.

This is not inherently unreasonable. Alignment at the top of a large organization is genuinely high-value work — imperfect information shared among intelligent people, correctly, costs less than decisions made on mismatched foundations. The problem is the fraction of that time spent rebuilding context that already exists: re-explaining backgrounds that were already explained, re-confirming facts that were already confirmed, re-establishing shared ground that had formed last week and since evaporated.

That fraction is not the cost of complexity. It is the cost of running a complex organization without infrastructure for its own cognition.

---

As a historian of everyday things, I tend to distrust accounts of technology that describe only what the tool can do. The interesting question is always what the tool does to the people working around it — and that question is usually answered years after the tool is adopted, by people who never saw it coming.

The last time organizations faced a knowledge-distribution problem at this scale, the answer was the vertical filing cabinet. When the Library Bureau began promoting it in the 1890s, it was sold as a space-saving device. What it actually did was transfer institutional memory out of the heads of senior clerks and into a physical system that any employee could query. Companies discovered, for the first time, that they could reassign a client relationship without a ritual handover, or dismiss a veteran without losing the business he carried. An efficiency product turned out to be an epistemic one. The historian JoAnne Yates called the shift that followed "control through communication" — a phrase worth remembering, because the tool we are now deploying will be asked to do something of the same order.

The dominant responses to the current alignment problem do not rise to that order. Some organizations are funneling everything into a single company-wide knowledge base and hoping retrieval will close the gaps. Others are leaning on a handful of unusually experienced managers to carry the organization across the spaces the system cannot reach. Neither response is wrong, exactly. Both are incomplete in the same way: they flatten categories of knowledge that are not the same kind of thing, and then ask one mechanism to hold all of them.

What the three layers actually need is three layers.

---

What those three layers are, in the end, is not a technology stack. It is a set of ownership commitments, and the ownership is what makes the rest of it work.

At the outermost ring sits the Corporate Knowledge Base. This is the organization's institutional memory: archived projects, policy decisions, filed reports, closed initiatives. It belongs to the company. When a new employee joins, the portions of this archive relevant to their role are surfaced into their personal space on day one — and at the same time, a small onboarding agent, drawn from the Corporate Knowledge Base, is installed temporarily into their Personal Shell. Through interactive dialogue, the agent walks them through the company's context: who reports to whom, what the last three quarters actually looked like, what the active initiatives are, where the written-down answers to their likely first questions can be found. The onboarding is not a document dump and not a series of recorded videos. It is the same induction a good team lead would run, but consistent, information-dense, and not dependent on whichever senior colleague happens to be free that week. When a new initiative launches, it draws context from here so the team does not rebuild the company's own history from scratch. When an initiative ends, its contents are folded back in.

In the middle sits the Initiative Knowledge Base. Each active project — a product launch, a due diligence, a regulatory filing — has its own bounded knowledge space, shared among the people working on it and sealed at the project's end. This is where the living state of current work actually exists: what is active, what has stalled, what the team has learned in the last two weeks. When the work is done, the base is closed, structured, and handed back up to the Corporate layer.

At the innermost ring sits the Personal Knowledge Base. This space belongs, exclusively and permanently, to the individual. It holds what a person actually carries with them through their working life: their private judgments about the people they work with, their notes on relationships and clients, the instincts they have developed over years and cannot fully articulate. What I call **Human Efforts** — the integrated capacity a professional brings to the moments that matter, most of it developed over years and most of it impossible to articulate even by the person who carries it — lives in this layer and only in this layer. No organizational agent reads it. No enterprise system indexes it. Information flows in freely; it flows outward only when the person chooses to submit something upward, into an initiative or a report.

Return to the Tech Demo. Imagine running it again, with this architecture already in place. Twenty-four hours before the session, the system compares the participant list against what can be inferred from past work: who has already configured this connector, who has the runtime environment installed, who has used the underlying tool in a prior initiative. The checks run in the background. I never see them. What each participant sees, a day before the session, is a quiet note in their own Personal Shell — not a document link, but a two-minute interactive walkthrough from an agent that has already identified, specifically for them, the one or two small things they are missing. One person gets the installation walkthrough. Another gets the connector configuration. A third, already set up, gets nothing. The next day, the forty-eight minutes that previously went to baseline questions are simply gone, because the baseline is already there.

The point is not that people have been spared the embarrassment of asking. The point is that the question of who did not know what never enters the room at all — not for the participants, not for me as the organizer, not for anyone above us who might later come looking. Ignorance alignment, in this architecture, does not mean mapping who is ignorant of what. It means making sure that by the time the conversation starts, the relevant ignorance has been quietly and privately resolved.

This is not a technology miracle, and anyone who has built such a system knows better than to present it as one. The architecture respects Human Sloppiness — the gap between what people can articulate and what they actually know — but it does not resolve it. People will skim their briefings. Files will be ingested into Personal Knowledge Bases the person never reads, leaving the system convinced they know things they do not. We honor Human Efforts, and we still need effort from humans — the pun is intentional. What the architecture does is clear the space around the effort, so the effort can go toward the part of the work only a human can do.

One commitment makes the whole structure trustworthy. The Personal Knowledge Base belongs to the person. It travels with them when they leave. No company mechanism can extract it against their will. This is not a feature to negotiate; it is the condition under which the other two layers become safe to use. I suspect that in the decade ahead, the question of what belongs to the employee and what belongs to the company will be among the more consequential unsettled questions in the employment relationship. The answer starts here.

---

Every organization is already paying the alignment tax. It shows up in the meeting that ran to catch-up instead of decisions, in the assistant who composes every email as if for an archive because the archive does not exist, in the capable professional who did not raise a hand because the cost of raising it was higher than the cost of staying lost. The tax is continuous and it is expensive. What is unusual about it is that nothing in return for it is ever booked.

The question worth asking is not how to make people faster, or meetings shorter, or documents more findable. It is prior to all of those:

Does your organization know what it doesn't know?

And if you had to answer that question right now — not next quarter, not after the next survey, but by the end of the afternoon — who would you even ask?
