## Article Pitches Identified

### PITCH 1 - "The Alignment Tax" [COMPLETED - PUBLISHED 2026-04-09]

**Argument:** Large organizations do not have an efficiency problem. They have an epistemic alignment problem with three layers: information misalignment, progress misalignment, and ignorance misalignment. The third is the most expensive because it is invisible to both sides (the curse of knowledge makes it invisible from the sender's side; the absence of knowledge makes it invisible from the receiver's). The solution is a three-layer knowledge architecture with distinct ownership commitments.

**Published title:** The Alignment Tax (working title; final title TBD by Vincent)

**Key concepts introduced:**
- The Alignment Tax (named framework)
- Three layers of misalignment: information, progress, ignorance
- "You cannot ask for what you do not know to ask for, and you cannot share what you do not realize others lack"
- "The curse of knowledge in everything leads to misalignment of ignorance everywhere"
- Three-layer knowledge architecture: Corporate KB / Initiative KB / Personal KB
- Asymmetric permeability (information flows into Personal KB freely; flows out only by choice)
- Data ownership as precondition, not feature
- Human Efforts (cross-referenced from Article 1/LinkedIn piece)
- Human Sloppiness (cross-referenced from Article 1/LinkedIn piece)

**Anchoring scenes used:**
- The cross-departmental meeting (composite, opening)
- Tech Demo for ~100 colleagues (Vincent's real experience, Section II + V)
- Vertical filing cabinet / JoAnne Yates "control through communication" (historian pivot)

**Data citations:**
- McKinsey (2013, 1,374 executives): 39% of working time in internal meetings
- Bain: ~40% of meeting time devoted to "informing" not deciding
- Asana (2023): 58-60% of knowledge workers' time on "work about work"

### PITCH 2 - "The Most Expensive Problem in Your Company Is the One You Can't Demonstrate"

**Argument:** There are two fundamentally different categories of AI deployment. Tool-layer AI (email drafting, slide generation) is visible, measurable, easy to demo. Cognitive-layer AI changes the background conditions under which every decision is made, and its value is invisible: it lives in decisions that didn't go wrong, meetings that ran to agenda, new executives who understood the company in weeks not months. While everyone watches the tool layer, the cognitive layer goes unbuilt.

**Anchor:** "People are convinced by tools, but understood by stories."

### PITCH 4 - "The Polanyi Problem" / merged with PITCH 11 "How to Think With AI"

**Argument:** Polanyi's tacit knowledge gap is the central unsolved problem in organizational AI. "Configure your rules" fails because people can't articulate their rules. The correct design pattern: let the system generate, let humans react to specifics, let the system calibrate from concrete feedback. At the personal level, this means exposing uncertainty produces better AI output than projecting competence.

**Anchor:** The "review and approve" interaction mode as the most important design pattern.

**Note from analysis:** Pitches 4 and 11 share the same root (tacit knowledge). Recommended writing as one piece: personal prompting as entry point, organizational design as the deeper argument.

**Status:** Not started.

### PITCH 5 - "The Invisible Half of AI Value"

**Argument:** Every organization measures the wrong thing. Tool-level metrics (email processing time reduced by X%) are the explicit half. Cognitive coordination value is the implicit half, fundamentally harder to demonstrate, fundamentally more valuable. The right metrics are passive, extracted from data the system already processes.

**Anchor:** The KPI framework from Doc 5 (information latency, epistemic alignment failure rate, decision capture rate, registry freshness).

**Status:** Not started. Some overlap with Pitch 2; may merge or sequence.

### PITCH 7 - "The Surveillance Trap"

**Argument:** The most common failure in org AI deployment is trust collapse, not technical failure. The moment employees perceive monitoring, they manage their input, corrupting the data, making insights worse than no insights. Personal information sovereignty is the political foundation.

**Anchor:** Asymmetric visibility design: your boss cannot see your blind spots through the system.

**Status:** Not started. Partially covered in Pitch 1's data ownership section.

### PITCH 8 - "The Zombie Project Problem: Where Organizational AI Actually Begins"

**Argument:** The chicken-and-egg problem of cognitive infrastructure. The system is least valuable at the start. Solution: redefine value. First output isn't insight, it's structured data flow. Highest-ROI first action: Initiative Registry cold start via email scanning. Most important byproduct: discovering zombie projects.

**Anchor:** Cold start sequence (scan email, infer 25 projects, send confirmation requests, discover cancelled project nobody was notified about). "In a typical 2,000-person company, the number of zombie projects usually shocks the COO."

## Key Decisions Made During Pitch 1 Writing

1. **Two sessions' outlines merged:** Another session produced a PIE Stack (Personal-Initiative-Enterprise) outline. We kept our Pitch 1 structure (problem-first) and grafted the best elements from the other session (named framework, adversarial framing, tribal knowledge observation).

2. **Meeting Room opening preserved over Tech Demo opening.** Meeting Room is composite/universal (every exec recognizes it). Tech Demo is specific/first-person (anchors the third layer). They work in sequence, not as alternatives.

3. **Human Efforts definition kept self-contained.** Even though it was defined in the prior LinkedIn article, this article re-explains it so it stands alone. HBR online version can hyperlink.

4. **"Not a Magic Solution" caveat included.** Placed in Section V as a strength, not a weakness. "We honor Human Efforts, and we still need effort from humans."

5. **Data ownership placed in Section V, not Close.** Data ownership is a claim; the close is a question. Separated for maximum impact on both.

6. **Historian pivot compressed to one example (vertical filing cabinet).** JoAnne Yates' "control through communication" serves as the bridge from historical precedent to current architecture.

7. **Ignorance alignment reframed as double-blind.** Not "people hide what they don't know" (that's one-sided). The real structure: the sender can't know what the receiver lacks, AND the receiver can't know what to ask for. Both sides are blind.

8. **System resolves ignorance without exposing it to anyone.** The Meeting Prep scenario: the system self-checks and privately resolves gaps. The organizer never sees who was ignorant. The participant never has to admit it publicly. The question of "who didn't know what" never enters the room.

9. **Vincent's final version substantially expanded the enterprise software paragraph** (Oracle/Salesforce/ClickUp), added the financial analyst example for Personal KB, and rewrote the close to include "fake it till you make it" as a callback.
