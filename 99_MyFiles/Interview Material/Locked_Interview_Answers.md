### 5. Influence - Belmont Legal Safeguard
**Snowflake dimensions covered:** Influence, Collaborate+Conflict, People, Problem Solving

**H:** I pushed through a legal safeguard for a six-figure horse transaction against the resistance of different stakeholder

**S:** At Belmont, we import competition horses from Europe. We had a horse worth around $160,000 to $180,000, and a former national team rider wanted his student to trial it. If the horse was injured or died during the trial, our entire cashflow would collaps. 

**O:** The industry runs on handshakes, and sales are normally arranged by the horse trainer. Both her and my superior wanted to proceed informally. 

 But I'd reviewed Florida law and knew that without a nominal transaction on paper, any agreement was unenforceable. If that horse was injured during the trial, our entire cash flow would collapse - we couldn't cover the other horses' boarding costs, couldn't buy new inventory, and couldn't afford the months a legal dispute would take.

**A:** I separated the primary issue from the secondary one. The trainer's resistance was real, but the business owner's sign-off was the gate. I focused there first - built out the financial analysis, walked him through what happens to our cash position if this horse is lost with no enforceable contract. He signed.

Then I went to the trainer. I understood his resistance - in this industry, asking someone to sign paperwork feels like saying you don't trust them. I made it clear this wasn't about him. He'd done nothing wrong. His priorities and the business's priorities just didn't align on this one point, and that's normal. I told him the contract wouldn't change his role - he still runs the horse side. And if the paperwork caused any delay, I'd personally cover the cost from my own income. He wasn't happy, but he understood it wasn't personal.

**R:** Contract was signed. The trial went forward. That buyer's student didn't take the horse, but the exposure generated word of mouth - we now have a buyer at $220,000. More importantly, every transaction since then follows this protocol. It's now standard practice.

**L:** Introducing process in a trust-based environment feels like an insult to the people in it. The key is separating the emotional resistance from the business logic and addressing each one on its own terms.

### 6. Leadership - Nanjing Lunch Reform
**Snowflake dimensions covered:** Leadership, Influence, Collaborate+Conflict, Impact

**H:** I led an institutional reform at a school in China where every level of the organization had a different reason to resist the change.

**S:** At Nanjing Foreign Language School, the leadership wanted to give students the freedom to use nearby restaurants as a lunch option. The cafeteria had been overloaded with nearly 5,000 people on campus. Quality was suffering, and cost inflated.

**O:** Leadership wanted the change. But frontline teachers felt they'd be the ones handling any incident. Parents were split between welcoming it and worrying about safety. And this wasn't a pilot - it would become long-term policy, so every stakeholder needed to be on board.

**A:** One argument wasn't going to move everyone, so I addressed each group separately.

I persuaded leadership to expand the student affairs office and reallocate part of the cafeteria budget to hire dedicated staff for off-campus lunch issues. This gave frontline teachers a clear answer: if something happens, someone else handles it, not you.

I know I did not eliminate the actual risk, but that's just the nature of our world. You do what you can control, and you build the safety net for what you can't.

**R:** The reform has been running for over three years with zero serious incidents. The contingency protocol is still working and getting improved every year - nothing ever escalated to hospitalization. Not to mention the school saved a few million every year from an overworked cafeteria.

**L:** Every group says no for a different reason. If you try one argument for everyone, you convince nobody. You have to understand the resistance from their perspective and address each piece on its own terms - which is exactly how I think about AI adoption. You can build the best tool in the world, but if you don't address each user group's specific resistance, it dies on arrival.

### 9. Failure - The Demo That Lost 50 Minutes (NovelOS Demo)
**Snowflake dimensions covered:** Failure, Self-awareness, AI deployment philosophy, People perception
**Status:** Last working version - L section may still be refined

**H:** I designed what I thought was a perfect AI demo for 50 colleagues. Then I watched 50 out of 60 minutes go to questions I never anticipated.

**S:** I'm part of a freelance writer/editor team for webnovel publications. Ever since I launched NovelOS - a local AI knowledge system that archives thousands of raw novel drafts into structured worldbuilding and plot tracking - I've become the AI guy on our editorial team. Everyone wanted one for themselves, so I prepared a tech demo for our writers and editors a few days ago.

**O:** I thought about everything. Privacy concerns, cross-book content contamination, IP protection, different comfort levels with AI, future compatibility with mainstream publishing infrastructure. I prepared thorough handouts, a concept cheat sheet, a Python script for automating folder setup, contingency plans and alternative APIs in case my audience couldn't access Claude Code. I ran simulation rehearsals. I genuinely believed I had covered every angle.

**A:** Within the first few minutes, the questions started. "How do I install Claude Code?" "Why do I have to pay five dollars?" "How do I open this setting in Obsidian?" "What is a plugin?" For 50 minutes, 50 capable senior editors and brilliant writers asked questions that had nothing to do with the demo itself. Each one of them was missing a different small piece - and none of those pieces were the ones I'd thought to cover. They were stuck on the floor beneath the floor I'd built on.

**R:** The good news - because the demo was so well structured and the prompt was robust, in the remaining 10 minutes everyone who followed along walked away with a working novel archive AI system. The product worked. But 80% of my carefully designed content - the lectures on prompt design, the vision for publishing infrastructure - was never delivered.

**L:** I had a blind spot. I designed the demo as if the room was full of versions of me - and it wasn't. People walked away confused on things I'd never thought to explain, because to me they were obvious. What I learned is that this isn't about them being unprepared, and it isn't about me being careless. It's that humans are sloppy by nature - we never know exactly what we need, and we never can fully articulate what we know. And that includes me. I can't see my own gaps from the inside any more than they could see theirs. So now I treat that human sloppiness, mine included, as the first design constraint of any system I build - not as a flaw to fix, but as the condition you design around. I wrote about this on LinkedIn in a piece called "The Alignment Tax."

**H:** "I nearly killed a six-figure deal and a relationship with a national team figure because I assumed my way of doing business applied in a new industry."

**S:** "At Belmont, we had a horse worth around $160,000 to $180,000. A former riding legend reached out to our trainer and wanted his student to trial it. I reviewed the law, saw the risk, and pushed for a full-fledged legal contract before the trial."

**O:** "In the horse world, many transactions are done over the handshake, with reputation as the safeguard instead of law. Asking someone to sign paperwork before a purchase is like accusing them of misconduct. I didn't know that. I came from environments where contracts are standard. I treated this like any other business transaction."

**A:** "Our horse trainer warned us. But I thought I was being responsible. I persuaded the business owner and pushed the contract through. The buyer's side took it as an insult. Not just a procedural inconvenience. An insult. We were close to losing not just this deal but the entire relationship with an industry legend whose network could make or break our reputation in that market."

**R:** "The trainer saved it. She went to the buyer and explained that I was new here and I simply didn't understand the norms. The buyer was understanding and forgiving. The deal went through. No price pressure. But that was the trainer's save, not mine."

**L:** "I learned that being right on the business logic doesn't mean I'm right about the room. When I enter a new environment, whether it's an industry, a department, or a culture, I observe and I ask to have some ground before I propose. I don't need a perfect answer, but that's no excuse to be arrogant or reckless. If I have the pleasure of working at SharkNinja, that's what I would always do. Before I build anything for any team, I'd make some cold calls, sit down and understand how they actually work, then try to drive humble changes."
