# The Alignment Tax

The meeting was scheduled for an hour. Eight people, three dialing in from regional offices. The agenda had four items. The last was the one that mattered — a resource allocation decision that would shape the next two quarters.

Twenty minutes in, someone from finance mentioned a number. It was not new. It had appeared in an email three weeks earlier, sent to a distribution list that included five of the eight people in the room. The other three — including the two regional leads whose input was the reason the meeting existed — had never received it.

The meeting paused. Someone shared the attachment. The finance lead explained context. A clarifying question led to a second, then a third. Twenty minutes passed. The conversation was useful, but it was not the conversation anyone had come to have.

The meeting ended. The allocation decision was deferred. No one described what happened as a failure, because it felt like a normal meeting. It was a normal meeting. That is the problem.

---

What occurred in that room was not a scheduling error or a communication lapse. It was a structural failure in how the organization distributes knowledge. It happens every day, in every company past a certain size, and it does not have a name. So give it one: the alignment tax.

The failure in that meeting had three layers, each with a different character and a different cost.

The first is information alignment — the gap between the people who hold a document and the people who need it. The finance memo existed. It had been sent. The failure was in distribution, invisible to both sides. This is the layer organizations recognize. They have tried to address it with shared drives, enterprise search, and knowledge bases, producing vast collections of documents technically accessible and practically unfindable. The residual cost lands on individuals — typically the assistants and operations staff who compose every email as if for an archive, push files to anyone who might need them, and send "just in case" copies they know will go unread. The missing infrastructure becomes a quiet tax paid by the people least positioned to refuse it.

The second is progress alignment. In any organization running dozens of parallel initiatives, no single place maintains a live picture of current status. Projects stall while the organization treats them as active. Projects finish while budget and attention keep flowing. Progress information is generated continuously but captured episodically, at review cycles too infrequent to reflect what is actually happening.

The third layer almost never gets named. Call it ignorance alignment: the gap between what a group needs to share in order to work at depth, and what each person actually arrives with. Not expertise. Not judgment. The baseline — the common floor of facts, definitions, procedural knowledge, and situational context that every substantive conversation assumes is in place before it begins.

Here is what this looks like. Last month I ran a one-hour technical session for roughly two hundred colleagues, demonstrating how to build a custom AI agent from scratch. The session had been carefully designed — a detailed prompt, a structured path for refining the agent in real time. Of the full hour, the part where I was demonstrating the core idea took twelve minutes.

The remaining forty-eight minutes went to questions that had nothing to do with the idea itself. Where do I install this? Can you send the access link? Which folder does this file go in? How do I configure the connector? Every one of these questions should have been resolved before the session began, by someone other than me, through some channel other than the demonstration itself.

Two hundred capable professionals, each missing a different small piece of context — not from lack of preparation, but because no part of the organization owned the job of making sure they arrived with the same floor under their feet. And what makes this layer expensive is what keeps it invisible. Most of the people who could not install the tool did not raise their hands. Asking a baseline question in front of senior colleagues — when everyone else appears to follow — is a professional risk people correctly calculate and decline to take. The twelve minutes of substance reached only the fraction of the audience who had already, somehow, found their way to the floor.

Research from McKinsey and Bain converges on a striking estimate: senior executives spend close to 40 percent of their working time in internal meetings, and roughly 40 percent of that meeting time goes not to decisions but to informing — synchronizing context, rebuilding shared ground, catching participants up on developments they missed. The arithmetic is plain: nearly one working day in five, for every senior leader, consumed by alignment work before any substantive discussion begins.

This is not inherently unreasonable. Alignment at the top of a complex organization is genuinely high-value work. The problem is the fraction of that time spent rebuilding context that already exists — re-explaining backgrounds already explained, re-confirming facts already confirmed, re-establishing shared ground that had formed last week and since evaporated. That fraction is not the cost of complexity. It is the cost of running a complex organization without infrastructure for its own cognition.

---

I study the microhistory of technology — not wars or states, but the everyday devices and infrastructure that quietly restructure how people live. How flush toilets reshaped domestic architecture. How gas stoves changed not just cooking but the rhythm of a household's day. My professional reflex, when I encounter any technology, is not to ask what it can do but what it does to the people working around it.

The last time organizations faced a knowledge-distribution problem at this scale, the answer was the vertical filing cabinet. When the Library Bureau began promoting it in the 1890s, it was sold as a space-saving device. What it actually did was transfer institutional memory out of the heads of senior clerks and into a physical system that any employee could query. Companies discovered they could reassign a client relationship without a ritual handover, or dismiss a veteran employee without losing the business he carried in his head. An efficiency product turned out to be an epistemic one. The historian JoAnne Yates called the shift that followed "control through communication." The phrase is worth remembering, because the tool we are now deploying will be asked to do something of the same order.

The dominant responses to the alignment problem do not rise to that order. Some organizations funnel everything into a single company-wide knowledge base and hope retrieval will close the gaps. Others lean on a handful of unusually experienced managers to carry the organization across the spaces the system cannot reach. Neither response is wrong. Both are incomplete in the same way: they flatten categories of knowledge that are not the same kind of thing, and then ask one mechanism to hold all of them.

What the three layers actually need is three layers.

At the outermost ring sits the Corporate Knowledge Base — the organization's institutional memory. Archived projects, policy decisions, closed initiatives. It belongs to the company. When a new employee joins, the portions relevant to their role are surfaced into their workspace, and an onboarding agent — drawn from this layer — walks them through context interactively: who reports to whom, what the last three quarters looked like, where the answers to their likely first questions live. Not a document dump. Not a series of recorded videos. The same induction a good team lead would run, but consistent, information-dense, and not dependent on whichever senior colleague happens to be free that week. When a new initiative launches, it draws context from here. When an initiative ends, its contents fold back in.

In the middle sits the Initiative Knowledge Base. Each active project — a product launch, a regulatory filing, a due diligence — has its own bounded knowledge space, shared among the people working on it. This is where the living state of current work exists: what is active, what has stalled, what the team has learned this week. When the work is done, the base is closed, structured, and handed up to the Corporate layer.

At the innermost ring sits the Personal Knowledge Base. This space belongs to the individual for the duration of their employment. It holds what a person actually carries through their working life: their private judgments, their notes on relationships and clients, the instincts they have developed over years. No organizational agent reads it. No enterprise system indexes it. Information flows in freely; it flows out only when the person chooses to contribute something upward, into an initiative or a report.

This layer exists to clear the ground. A financial analyst whose real value is the conclusion she draws from a dataset currently spends hours building the spreadsheet, formatting the report, and converting a judgment she already holds into language that satisfies the document's structure. The formatting, the retrieval, the mechanical act of verbalization — none of it requires her. It requires a person only because no alternative has existed. The Personal Knowledge Base, configured to her patterns, absorbs the mechanical substrate so that her cognitive space is freed for the work that actually demands her: the analysis, the judgment, the conclusion that no system can reach on her behalf.

One commitment makes this architecture trustworthy. The company never reads the Personal Knowledge Base. When an employee departs, the corporate-context portion — client preferences, internal dynamics, proprietary workflows — is deleted. The architecture does not pretend to own what lives in the person's head, and it does not let the person walk away with what belongs to the company. The question of what, precisely, belongs to whom will be among the more consequential unsettled questions in the employment relationship over the decade ahead. But the principle is the precondition: without the guarantee of privacy, the other two layers lose the trust that makes them safe to use.

The architecture respects what humans cannot cleanly express — but it does not assume humans will use it perfectly. People will skim their briefings. Files will be ingested that the person never reads, leaving the system convinced they know things they do not. We always know more than we can articulate, we always need more than we can describe, and any system that demands perfect specification as its operating condition will fail. What the architecture does is clear the space around the effort, so the effort can go toward the part of the work only a human can do.

---

Every organization is already paying the alignment tax. It shows up in the meeting that ran to catch-up instead of decisions, in the assistant who composes every email as if for an archive because the archive does not exist, in the capable professional who did not raise a hand because the cost of raising it was higher than the cost of staying lost. The tax is continuous and it is expensive. Nothing in return for it is ever booked.

The question worth asking is not how to make people faster, or meetings shorter, or documents more findable. It is prior to all of those.

Does your organization know what it doesn't know?

And if you had to answer that question by the end of the afternoon — not next quarter, not after the next survey — who would you even ask?
