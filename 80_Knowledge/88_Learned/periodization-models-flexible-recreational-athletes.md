# Periodization Models for Recreational Athletes with Unpredictable Schedules

## Why Strict Linear Periodization Is Not Superior for Recreational Trainees

- Meta-analyses consistently show no significant difference between linear periodization (LP) and undulating periodization (UP) for strength or hypertrophy in untrained/recreational populations.
- The 2026 Frontiers meta-analysis confirmed: LP and DUP produce comparable outcomes in upper and lower body strength. Trained individuals may benefit slightly more from undulating schemes, but the effect is absent in recreational trainees.
- For recreational athletes, any progressive overload stimulus is sufficient. The specific periodization model matters far less than consistency, adherence, and adequate volume.
- Strict LP requires predictable training availability (3-4 fixed sessions/week for 4-6 week blocks), which breaks down with irregular recreational schedules.

## Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)

- DUP rotates training stimuli (e.g., hypertrophy/strength/power) across sessions within the same week rather than across multi-week blocks.
- Advantage for recreational athletes: each session is self-contained. Missing a session does not derail a multi-week progression, because the rotation simply continues at the next session.
- Flexible DUP (FDUP) lets the athlete choose which stimulus to train on a given day based on readiness, equipment availability, or preference. Research on trained powerlifters showed FDUP produced comparable results to fixed DUP with greater autonomy.
- Practical implementation: assign 3 session types (e.g., heavy/moderate/light) and rotate in order regardless of which calendar day they fall on.

## Autoregulated Periodization

- **APRE (Autoregulatory Progressive Resistance Exercise):** uses performance on a set-to-failure to determine load for the next set. The 2025 network meta-analysis found APRE produced the largest effect size for maximal strength enhancement compared to RPE-based and velocity-based methods.
- **RPE/RIR-based autoregulation:** prescribes target RPE (e.g., RPE 8 = 2 reps in reserve) rather than fixed percentages. Allows automatic load adjustment for good/bad days without recalculating 1RM.
- **Velocity-Based Training (VBT):** uses bar speed to regulate load. Effective but requires equipment (linear position transducer or app). Ranked below APRE but above RPE in the 2025 meta-analysis.
- All autoregulated methods significantly outperform fixed percentage-based training (PBRT) for strength in the meta-analysis. The advantage is strongest for trainees with variable readiness, which describes recreational athletes precisely.

## Recommended Hybrid Model for Recreational Multi-Sport Athletes

- **Structure:** Flexible DUP rotation (heavy/moderate/light) with RPE-based load selection within each session.
- **Progression:** session-to-session, not week-to-week. Track loads per session type and aim to progress within each type independently.
- **Readiness integration:** HRV gate determines whether to proceed with the planned session type or swap to a lower-demand type (e.g., swap heavy day to moderate if HRV is suppressed).
- **Deload trigger:** session-count-based (every 12-15 hard sessions) rather than calendar-based, to accommodate schedule variability.
- This model tolerates missed sessions, variable weekly frequency, and multi-sport fatigue accumulation without requiring reprogramming.

## Key Numbers

- APRE effect size for strength: SMD 1.29 (large) vs PBRT in the 2025 meta-analysis
- RPE-based: SMD 0.87 (moderate-large) vs PBRT
- DUP vs LP difference for recreational trainees: not statistically significant (multiple meta-analyses)
- Minimum effective dose for strength maintenance during disrupted weeks: 1 session/week at previous intensity, even with reduced volume
