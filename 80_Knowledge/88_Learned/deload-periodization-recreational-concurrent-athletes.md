# Deload Strategies for Recreational Concurrent Athletes

## Why Traditional Deload Cycles Fail for Recreational Schedules

- The standard 3:1 or 4:1 (load:deload) paradigm assumes consistent weekly volume and progressive overload across the loading block. Recreational athletes with variable schedules already have involuntary low-volume weeks (travel, work spikes, life events).
- Calendar-based deloading every 4-6 weeks can misfire: the athlete may deload after an already-light week, or miss the window when fatigue actually peaks.
- Concurrent athletes accumulate fatigue from multiple modalities (strength, climbing, equestrian) that don't follow a single periodization timeline. A deload timed to the strength cycle may coincide with peak climbing volume.

## Reactive vs Proactive Deload Triggers

- The Delphi consensus (2023) found practitioners use both proactive (pre-planned) and reactive (autoregulated) approaches, often in combination.
- For recreational concurrent athletes, reactive triggers are more appropriate because training load is inherently non-linear. Effective triggers include:
  - HRV suppression below SWC for 3+ consecutive days
  - Subjective fatigue: RPE creep (same loads feel 1-2 points harder)
  - Performance stall: missing rep targets on 2+ consecutive sessions
  - Persistent soreness or joint aches lasting >72h
  - Sleep quality decline for 3+ nights

## Session-Count-Based vs Calendar-Based Deload

- For non-linear schedules, counting training sessions is more valid than counting calendar weeks. A "deload after every 12-15 high-intensity sessions" approach adapts to actual training stress regardless of calendar gaps.
- The cross-sectional survey (2024) found mean deload frequency of every 5.6 +/- 2.3 weeks, but with high individual variability. Session-count naturally produces similar periodicity when training is consistent, but self-corrects during irregular periods.
- Hybrid approach: set a session-count trigger (e.g., 12-15 hard sessions) AND a calendar ceiling (e.g., never exceed 6 weeks without deload regardless of session count).

## What to Do During Deload

- Consensus: reduce volume (fewer sets per session, fewer sessions), maintain or slightly reduce intensity (load on bar). Dropping intensity while keeping volume is less effective for preserving strength adaptations.
- Typical prescription: reduce total weekly sets by 40-60%, maintain loads at 85-95% of recent working weights, reduce proximity to failure (stop 3-4 reps short instead of 1-2).
- Duration: 5-7 days (mean 6.4 days in the 2024 survey). One week is practical and sufficient.
- Training frequency stays the same or reduces by one session. Complete rest is not recommended, as it risks detraining and disrupts routine.
- For concurrent athletes: deload the primary fatiguing modality (usually strength) while maintaining skill-based activities (climbing technique, equestrian) at low intensity.

## Caution: Deload Timing and Hypertrophy

- One 2024 RCT found a mid-program deload slightly reduced lower-body strength gains vs continuous training, though hypertrophy was unaffected. This suggests deloads should be triggered by genuine fatigue signals rather than applied preventatively when adaptation is still progressing.
