Remington sold the typewriter (1870s) as a tool for writers and telegraph operators to write faster. It accidentally created a new occupational category (typist-stenographer) and restructured office labor.

Before typewriter: "clerk" was a male entry-level role that served as pathway to management. Copying letters was how you learned the business.

After typewriter: letter-writing split into drafting (male managers dictate) and transcription (female typists execute mechanically). By 1900, 77% of US stenographers/typists were women.

Key second-order effect: the clerk role lost its "pathway to management" semantics because business knowledge no longer flowed through the physical act of copying. The typewriter invented the career ceiling.

This is a clean example of: a tool adopted for productivity reasons that restructured the nature of work and the social relationships around it, in ways nobody planned.
