# Meal Timing, Blood Glucose, and Resistance Training — Research Summary (2023–2025)

## Q1: Fed vs. Fasted Resistance Training — Does Blood Glucose State Matter for Performance?

**Short answer: No significant performance difference.**

- Vieira et al. (2025) — RCT, 12 weeks, twice-weekly RT: Both fasted and fed groups showed similar quadriceps hypertrophy (+1.21 vs +1.18 cm), dynamic strength (bench press, knee extension), and muscle power. No significant between-group differences.
  - *International Journal of Sport Nutrition and Exercise Metabolism*, vol. 35 no. 4, pp. 291–302. PMID 40335157.

- Vieira, Macedo, Cadore et al. (2025) — Systematic review + meta-analysis: Across multiple RCTs, fasted vs. fed RT showed no significant differences in fat-free mass, muscle hypertrophy, or maximal strength. One study showed greater maximal strength gains in the fed state.
  - *Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies*, 2025 Dec:45:99–109. PMID 41316673.

- Seow et al. (2025) — Meta-analysis (28 RCTs, n=302): Fasted exercise associated with elevated serum glucose and insulin compared to fed exercise, and reduced free fatty acids post-exercise. Fat oxidation higher in fasted state acutely.
  - *ScienceDirect* (Metabolism journal family). PMID 39921164.

**Key nuance:** Elevated blood glucose in the fed state does not translate into measurable RT performance gains over fasted. The glycogen stores available from prior meals appear sufficient for moderate-volume RT bouts either way.

## Q2: Optimal Pre-Exercise Meal Timing Window

No 2023–2025 RCT directly tested 1 hr vs. 2 hr vs. 3 hr windows for resistance training specifically. The evidence base for the optimal window derives from older literature and the ISSN position stand:

- ISSN position stand (reference standard): A high-carb, low-fat, moderate-protein meal 3 hours before exercise optimizes substrate availability. Meals consumed 1–2 hours before are acceptable but may cause GI discomfort for some.
- For RT specifically, the 2025 meta-analyses above suggest that timing matters less than total daily nutrient adequacy.
- Pre-workout multi-ingredient supplement vs. carbohydrate alone (2025 JISSN RCT): Similar resistance training outcomes in middle-aged adults regardless of formula — suggesting carbohydrate timing's impact on RT is modest.
  - Tandfonline JISSN, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/15502783.2025.2519515.

**Practical guidance from literature:** 1–3 hours pre-RT is the conventional window; no strong evidence that 1 hr vs. 3 hr makes a meaningful difference for RT performance in healthy adults.

## Q3: Meal Timing and Hypertrophy Outcomes

- Casuso & Goossens (2025) — Systematic review + meta-analysis (*Nutrients*, June 2025): Protein timing (pre vs. post exercise) does not importantly modify exercise-induced changes in lean body mass. Upper vs. lower limb strength may respond differently (pre-training protein showed greater leg press gains), but evidence is insufficient for firm conclusions.
  - *Nutrients*, 2025, 17(13):2070. PMID 40647175. PMC12250900.

**Bottom line:** Hypertrophy is driven by total daily protein and training stimulus. Timing (pre vs. post, fed vs. fasted) has negligible independent effect on muscle growth outcomes.

## Q4: Evening Training (After Dinner) — Pre-Training Nutrition

- Mattsson, Edin, Trinh et al. (2025) — RCT, male endurance cyclists: Post-exercise carbohydrate ingestion after high-intensity evening exercise markedly reduced morning glucose tolerance. Pre-exercise CHO ingestion preserved better nocturnal glucose regulation.
  - *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition*, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/15502783.2025.2494839. PMID 40259503. PMC12016275.

- Delaying post-exercise carbohydrate impairs next-day exercise capacity but not muscle glycogen (2024 PubMed 39263899): Immediate post-exercise refueling is recommended if next-day performance matters.

**Practical guidance:** For evening RT after dinner, the meal itself serves as adequate pre-training fuel. Post-exercise nutrition timing matters more for recovery and next-day readiness than for the evening session itself. Avoid large post-workout carb loads if blood glucose regulation overnight is a concern.

## Q5: Climbing-Specific Nutrition and Meal Timing

No 2023–2025 RCTs directly tested pre-exercise meal timing on grip endurance or climbing route performance.

Closest evidence:
- Seifert et al. (2024) — Physiological characteristics, dietary intake, and supplement use in sport climbing: Carbohydrate needs estimated at 3–7 g/kg/day. Most climbers consume inadequate carbohydrates.
  - *Current Nutrition Reports*, 2024. PMID 38112939. DOI: 10.1007/s13668-023-00511-x.

- Nutritional considerations for female rock climbers (2023, *Journal of Science in Sport and Exercise*): Highlights energy availability deficits and carbohydrate underfeeding as key performance limiters.
  - DOI: 10.1007/s42978-023-00267-4.

**Inference from general exercise science:** Climbing is glycolytically demanding (sustained grip endurance, repeated bouldering routes). Being in a euglycemic or mildly fed state before a climbing session (1–3 hrs post-meal) is likely preferable to fasted. No direct RCT evidence for climbing-specific meal timing exists as of 2025.
