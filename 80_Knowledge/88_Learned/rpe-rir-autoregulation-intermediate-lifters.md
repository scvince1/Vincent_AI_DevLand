# RPE/RIR Autoregulation — Intermediate & Recreational Lifters: Research Summary (2023–2025)

## Q1. Accuracy of Intermediate Lifters vs. Advanced Lifters

**Refalo et al. (2024)** — *Journal of Strength & Conditioning Research*, 38(3):e78–e85  
PMID: 37967832  
- 24 resistance-trained males and females; bench press at 75% 1RM
- Raw mean RIR accuracy: -0.17 ± 1.00 reps (1-RIR target); absolute accuracy: 0.65 ± 0.78 reps
- Minor systematic tendency for **underprediction** (i.e., stopped slightly earlier than target)
- No relationship found between accuracy and sex, years of RT experience, or relative strength
- **Implication:** Within resistance-trained populations, experience level within that range does not strongly predict accuracy; the key divide is trained vs. untrained

**Androulakis-Korakakis et al. (2023)** — PubMed 37036795  
- 27 men, 31 women; single-joint machine exercises (biceps curl, triceps pushdown, seated row)
- Accuracy improved when closer to failure and in later sets
- Training experience and sex did NOT significantly influence accuracy on machine-based exercises
- **Implication:** Machine-based exercises may be easier to estimate than free weights; experience effect weaker than often assumed

**Scoping review:** Ramos-Campo et al. (2024) — *Perceptual and Motor Skills*  
PMC: 11127506  
- 31 studies, N=855 participants; RIR scales "contextually feasible and useful" for prescribing RT intensity
- Novice lifters should practice RIR recording but not rely solely on it for load prescription until accuracy improves

## Q2. RPE-Based Programming vs. Percentage-Based for Hypertrophy

**Refalo et al. (2024)** — *Journal of Sport Sciences* (Taylor & Francis), published Feb 2024  
PMID: 38393985  
- 8-week RCT in resistance-trained males and females
- Lower limbs randomized: failure vs. 1–2 RIR (leaving reps in reserve)
- Result: **Similar quadriceps hypertrophy** between conditions
- Supports RIR-based prescription as hypertrophy-equivalent to failure-based training

**Nuzzo et al. (2025) — Network Meta-Analysis** — *Sports Medicine* or equivalent  
PMC: 12336695  
- Autoregulated RT (APRE, RPE, VBRT) significantly outperforms percentage-based RT for **maximal strength**
- APRE ranked highest; RPE second; VBRT third; PBRT lowest
- Hypertrophy: no systematic disadvantage for autoregulated methods vs. fixed %

## Q3. Known Failure Modes

**Bartolomei et al. (2024)** — *Journal of Strength & Conditioning Research*, Sept 2024  
PMC: 11343444 / PMID: 38814694  
- 24 resistance-trained men, 10-week perceived-recovery-based autoregulation program
- Autoregulated group had higher total volume but **no difference in muscle size, lean mass, strength, or power**
- Failure mode identified: subjects **overestimate recovery**, leading to inflated session RPE and inconsistent overload stimulus

**Validity review: Moran-Navarro et al. (2024)** — *Perceptual and Motor Skills* or similar  
PMC: 11569527 / PMID: 38910451  
- 18 studies reviewed; moderate positive correlations between RPE, %1RM, and movement velocity
- Notes that **motivation, training status, and environmental conditions** all distort RPE; professionals must account for these
- Systematic underestimation more common at **higher RIR targets** (far from failure) — accuracy collapses beyond 4+ RIR

**Summary of documented failure modes:**
1. Overestimation of recovery → sandbagging load selection
2. Underestimation of effort at high RIR (far from failure) — accuracy degrades
3. Motivation/environmental confounds distorting perception
4. Less accurate on multi-joint free weight movements vs. machines

## Q4. Recommended Modifications for RPE Newcomers

From Ramos-Campo et al. (2024) scoping review (PMC 11127506) and Refalo et al. (2024):
- **Practice recording first:** Have novices record RIR without acting on it; follow up with a failure set to check calibration
- **Use RPE ranges, not point targets:** e.g., "RPE 7–8" rather than "RPE 8" to allow error tolerance
- **Prioritize compound movements progressively:** Start with machines (easier to estimate), add free weights as calibration improves
- **Closer to failure = better accuracy:** Keep RIR target at ≤3 for practical reliability; >4 RIR predictions are unreliable
- Moran-Navarro et al. (2024) specifically recommend **familiarization protocols** before RPE-guided load prescription

## Q5. RPE Validity Across Different Locations and Equipment

**Moran-Navarro et al. (2024)** — systematic review, PMC 11569527  
- RPE scales show moderate-to-strong correlations with %1RM and movement velocity across exercise types
- RPE constitutes a "valid, economic, and practical tool for assessing RT load progression"
- **Equipment independence is a feature:** RPE measures internal load, not external load — inherently accounts for machine variation, unfamiliar equipment, fatigue state, travel

**Network meta-analysis (Nuzzo et al., 2025, PMC 12336695):**  
- RPE-based approaches consistently produced progressive overload in studies conducted across varied lab settings
- Velocity-based training (VBT) is more objective but requires hardware; RPE is the practical equivalent for non-lab settings

**Practical conclusion from literature:** RPE is *more* robust across equipment changes than percentage-based programming, which assumes stable equipment and calibrated 1RM — both of which break down in variable training environments.
