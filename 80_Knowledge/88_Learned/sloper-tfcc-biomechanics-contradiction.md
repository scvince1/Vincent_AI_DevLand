# Sloper TFCC Biomechanics: Why the General Advice Is Wrong for Heavy Climbers

## The Contradiction
General advice: "slopers safer than crimps." This is A2 PULLEY advice, NOT TFCC advice.

## Why Slopers Load TFCC
1. Friction-dependent grip → wrist cannot flex freely → compensatory wrist extension under load
2. Ulnocarpal compression + DRUJ stress → TFCC loaded in compression AND traction simultaneously
3. Primary TFCC stressors: repetitive axial loads with hand in ulnar deviation + pronation — exactly what slopers demand

## Why Worse at 91kg + Small Hands
- Absolute ulnocarpal load scales linearly with bodyweight (TFCC carries ~20% of wrist axial load)
- Dynamic ulnar variance increases with grip force in pronation (+1.95mm mean) → ulnocarpal load jumps to 35-40%
- Smaller hands = fewer fingers/less palm contacts optimal friction zone → compensatory ulnar deviation/wrist extension to "drape" more surface
- At 91kg on sloper: A2 pulley forces 400-600N approach documented failure thresholds

## Correct Grip Hierarchy for This User (TFCC Priority)
1. Jug (safest — full hand contact, minimal wrist deviation)
2. Open-hand on positive edges
3. Crimp (control volume, but pulley stress, not TFCC stress)
4. Sloper (AVOID — highest TFCC risk, injury history on this grip type)
