The household refrigerator (1920s-30s) was marketed as a labor-saving device. It actually transformed the nature of domestic procurement labor.

Before refrigerator: daily shopping routes, 5-10 small vendors, purchasing was both labor and social/information network (price comparison, credit relationships, neighborhood news).

After refrigerator: weekly bulk shopping became possible, which enabled the supermarket model (A&P, Piggly Wiggly). Shopping shifted from continuous micro-adjustments based on experience to a single weekly planning task (shopping lists, pre-planned menus, inventory management) - a new form of abstract cognitive labor.

Cowan's key finding: despite refrigerators, washing machines, and vacuum cleaners, American housewives' weekly housework hours barely decreased from 1920-1960. Standards rose invisibly to absorb the "saved" time (more complex menus, more frequent laundry, higher cleanliness standards).

Relevance: "labor-saving technology that doesn't actually save labor" is a recurring pattern. AI may do the same in organizations if the freed capacity is absorbed by rising expectations rather than redirected to higher-value work.
