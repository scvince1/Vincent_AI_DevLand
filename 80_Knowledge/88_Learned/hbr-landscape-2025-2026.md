## Key HBR articles in the space (as of early 2026)

1. "How Gen AI Could Change the Value of Expertise" - Fuller et al., Mar 2025
2. "When Every Company Can Use the Same AI Models, Context Becomes a Competitive Advantage" - Murty & Kumar, Feb 2026
3. "AI Doesn't Reduce Work - It Intensifies It" - Ranganathan & Ye, Feb 2026
4. "Strategy in an Era of Abundant Expertise" - Yerramilli-Rao et al., Mar 2025
5. "The 'Last Mile' Problem Slowing AI Transformation" - Lakhani et al., Mar 2026

## Cross-cutting findings

1. The tacit knowledge gap is underreported. HBR #5 mentions "tribal knowledge protection" as one of seven friction points but treats it as a bullet item, not a thesis. Nobody tells the story from the perspective of someone who actually confronted their own tacit knowledge while building a system.

2. HBR's strongest pieces use named frameworks readers can carry into meetings ("work graph," "learning curve / earning curve," "seven frictions"). Unnamed proposals get understood but not adopted.

3. HBR #3 ("AI Doesn't Reduce Work") proves counterintuitive findings land hard. HBR #5 ("Last Mile") proves executives are hungry for diagnostic frameworks. Both are allies, not competitors, to Vincent's articles.

## Implications for Vincent's writing
- Always name the framework (e.g. "The Alignment Tax")
- Open with failure/pain, not success
- Frame adversarially where appropriate (teams operating on contradictory beliefs)
- Keep limitation sections to one paragraph, not full sections
