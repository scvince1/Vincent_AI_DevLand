# Competitive Positioning: "Adjacent Not Competing" Framing

**Capture date:** 2026-04-11
**Tags:** competitive-strategy, positioning, B2B, SaaS, go-to-market, framing

---

## Summary

When a large incumbent appears to compete with your product, the instinct is to attack head-on or to retreat. A third path is to find the **dimensional cut** where you operate in a genuinely complementary space. The key is to make that cut *structural*, not aspirational — it must be grounded in a real difference in data substrate, customer motion, or measurement frame, not just word-smithing.

---

## Core Pattern

**The "adjacent not competing" framing works when:**
1. The incumbent's data substrate is fundamentally different from yours (owned vs. earned, inbound vs. outbound, existing customers vs. never-customers).
2. The incumbent's success metric is orthogonal to yours (engagement rate vs. early-warning detection).
3. You can state the complementarity in a single sentence the incumbent would not dispute.

**The canonical landing line (from the Salesforce Agentforce case):**
> "Agentforce is the voice of the consumers you already know. Our dashboard is the voice of the consumers you don't."

This works because:
- Agentforce operates on owned-system data only: support tickets, purchase history, SKU metadata. It does NOT ingest Reddit, Amazon reviews, YouTube comments, TikTok, or any external unstructured consumer text.
- The population Agentforce sees is structurally self-selected: consumers who (a) chose to contact the company AND (b) stayed engaged long enough for the agent to respond. Everyone who gave up without contacting, returned via retailer, or posted on Reddit without calling customer service is invisible to Agentforce by design.
- Industry rule-of-thumb (Technical Assistance Research Program, widely cited): only ~4-6% of dissatisfied customers actually contact the company. The "96% silent majority" is outside the CRM entirely.

## Analogous Applications in Other Industries

| Industry | Incumbent (what they see) | Complementary layer (what they miss) |
|---|---|---|
| Healthcare / pharma | Patient records, EHR claims data — people who came in | Public forum health discussions (Reddit health boards, condition communities) — patients who self-managed or switched |
| Financial services | Transaction history, CRM, inbound support — existing account holders | Social/forum discussions of people comparing banks or brokerages *before* opening an account |
| Hospitality / travel | Booking system, loyalty program, post-stay surveys — guests who stayed and responded | Review platforms, travel forums — guests who chose a competitor, or guests who stayed but never took the survey |

In each case, the incumbent's data is *complete within its population* but the population itself is a biased sample. The dimensional cut is always: **owned/inbound/existing vs. earned/outbound/never-yet**.

---

## How to Check Whether the Framing Is Defensible

A framing like this is **defensible** when all three hold:
1. **Structural, not aspirational**: the data substrates are genuinely non-overlapping (verify by checking the incumbent's own product documentation for what data sources they ingest).
2. **The incumbent wouldn't sue you for saying it**: the claim is that the incumbent does X well and you do Y, not that the incumbent does X badly. If your framing relies on calling the incumbent's product defective, you've crossed from "adjacent" to "attack."
3. **The gap is load-bearing for the customer**: there must be a real buyer pain that falls in the gap. If nobody cares about the unserved population, the complementarity framing has no commercial hook.

**Red flags (wishful thinking signals):**
- The incumbent has recently announced they are expanding into your exact substrate (check their release notes and roadmap announcements).
- Your framing requires the buyer to believe your data is inherently more valuable than the incumbent's — that's an attack, not a complement.
- You can only make the framing work with a very narrow slice of the incumbent's customer base.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. The strongest "adjacent not competing" arguments are measurement arguments, not product-quality arguments: "we measure something different," not "we do it better."
2. The landing line must be endorsable in principle by the competitor — if they would object to it, it's a competitive attack, not a complementary framing.
3. Always verify the incumbent's actual data substrate before finalizing the framing — it must reflect their real product scope, not a strawman.
