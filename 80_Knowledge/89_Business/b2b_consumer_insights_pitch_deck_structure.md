# B2B Consumer Insights Pitch Deck: Structural Skeleton

**Capture date:** 2026-04-11
**Tags:** B2B, pitch-deck, SaaS, go-to-market, narrative-structure, consumer-insights, sales-deck

---

## Summary

The genre conventions for B2B SaaS sales decks (not investor decks) when selling to a C-suite consumer insights buyer. Distilled from 2025-2026 B2B deck guidance and applicable to any consumer intelligence or text analytics product pitch.

---

## Core Framework: Problem → Wedge → Proof

The fundamental skeleton that high-performing B2B SaaS sales pitches follow:

1. **Problem (persona pain in their own voice):** Make buyers viscerally feel the pain of the status quo before showing how your product eliminates it. For a CGO audience this means: show what Monday morning looks like for the consumer insights manager today — not a market-size slide, but a workflow vignette.

2. **Wedge (what only you do):** Introduce your product as the dimensional shift, not as a feature list. The hero of the narrative is the shift in how the audience understands their own situation, not the product name.

3. **Proof (specific external authority + audit trail):** Credibility via concrete external references: case studies with names and numbers, technical badges (green test suites), authority signals (CPSC, Consumer Reports, Gartner). Not self-congratulation.

## Section-by-Section Skeleton

| Section | Purpose | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Executive TL;DR | One-sentence value proposition in buyer's language | Opens with persona vocabulary, not vendor vocabulary |
| 2. Status quo pain (before) | Workflow vignette — what the persona does today | Before side is more detailed than after side — force the audience to feel friction |
| 3. The invisible gap | Why the current approach misses something structural | Structural argument, not a product-quality attack |
| 4. The after vignette | Same workflow, with the tool | Keep short — transformation, not feature tour |
| 5. Wedge feature (1) | The first of 3 features that drive retention | Aspect-level drill-down with traceable quotes |
| 6. Wedge feature (2) | Second retention driver | Cross-platform signal confirmation |
| 7. Wedge feature (3) | Third retention driver | Novelty / rising-negative alerts |
| 8. External case study | Third-party authority | CPSC-level, not a customer testimonial |
| 9. Technical badge | Proof of claims | Green test suite; edge cases named and passed |
| 10. Out-of-scope honesty | What you don't do | Builds trust; signals you understand their real alternatives |
| 11. Customer story / quote | Social proof | Specific numbers, not generic praise |
| 12. Competitive positioning | Complementary framing | "Adjacent not competing" — name 1-2 incumbents, use their own gap admissions |
| 13. Call to action | Next step | One specific ask, not a menu |
| 14. Appendix | Methodology depth | For technical reviewers; not shown in the main pitch |

**Optimal length:** 14 slides/sections is the B2B SaaS high-performer consensus (Kolide research, widely cited).

## Specific Tropes to Know

**"Before/after" as primary visual device:**
- Before: intentionally more detailed than after. Spell out every friction point (manual tagging, Google Sheets, 500 reviews per SKU, takes 3 days). This forces the audience to feel the pain rather than just hear about it.
- After: brief. "She clicks Product Analysis, picks the SKU, sees the aspect trend with 47 traceable quotes. Google Sheet killed."

**"Live demo" or "judge-submitted text" slide:**
- For technical judges or skeptical buyers: a slot in the demo where the reviewer submits their own text and watches the NLP pipeline handle it in real time.
- Purpose: removes the "could have cherry-picked the demo data" objection.

**Demo narrative = 3 features, not a product tour:**
- Pick the 3 features that drive the strongest customer retention or that address the top-3 objections. Not a comprehensive feature tour.
- For consumer insights: (a) aspect-level sentiment with drill-through, (b) cross-platform confirmation, (c) novelty/rising-negative alerts.

**Proof hierarchy (CGO audience):**
1. External authority with numbers (CPSC record, Consumer Reports, third-party case study).
2. Technical badge (green tests, edge cases named and passed).
3. Operator resonance (buyer's own language fed back to them).

**What to omit in a pitch artifact (not a contract deck):**
- Roadmap promises
- Pricing
- Team slide
- Market size slide (investor deck, not sales deck)

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Opening with competitive gap bullets | Buyer hasn't yet felt the pain you're addressing; the gaps don't land without the before-vignette |
| Closing with the demo | CGO audience wants the workflow value proposition first; the technical proof ("tests are green") should close, not open |
| "Social listening platform" as category label | Vendor-speak; signals you don't know your buyer's mental model |
| Wall-of-features structure | Makes it a product catalog, not a transformation pitch |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. A sales deck is a workflow transformation pitch, not a feature catalog. Lead with the persona's Monday morning pain.
2. The "before" is more important than the "after" — spend more words on the friction than on the solution.
3. For a technical/skeptical buyer, the live demo with judge-submitted text is the highest-trust proof format.
4. 14 sections is the sweet spot. Each section has one job. No padding.
