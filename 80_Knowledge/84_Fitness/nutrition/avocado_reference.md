## Why Avocado is Considered "Quality Fat"

- **MUFA-dominant, oleic-acid-rich.** ~64% of avocado fat is monounsaturated, with oleic acid (the same fatty acid that defines olive oil) making up ~59% of total fat. This is the structural reason avocado is grouped nutritionally with olive oil and nuts rather than with butter.
- **Cardiovascular evidence.** Hass Avocado Board / NIH-funded clinical trials have shown that substituting one avocado/day for refined-carb or saturated-fat foods lowers LDL-C and oxidized LDL without raising HDL, similar in magnitude to MUFA-substitution effects from the Mediterranean-diet literature. The 2022 Wang et al. *Journal of the American Heart Association* prospective cohort (~110,000 adults, 30 yrs) found ≥2 servings/wk of avocado associated with ~16–22% lower CVD risk.
- **Phytosterol bonus.** Avocado contains ~76 mg β-sitosterol per 100 g — phytosterols compete with cholesterol for intestinal absorption, an additional LDL-lowering mechanism beyond fat composition.
- **Lipid-soluble vitamin carrier.** The fat matrix dramatically improves absorption of fat-soluble carotenoids/vitamins from co-eaten foods (Unlu et al. 2005: adding avocado to salsa increased lycopene/β-carotene absorption 2.6–4.4x; adding to salad increased α/β-carotene absorption ~7–15x). Worth pairing with tomato, leafy greens, carrots, sweet potato.

## K:Na Ratio and Hypertension Context

Avocado has one of the **most extreme K:Na ratios in the food supply: ~63:1** (507 mg K vs 8 mg Na per 100 g).

For comparison:
- Banana: ~360:1 K:Na — but lower absolute K density
- Spinach (raw): ~7:1
- Salted nuts: <1:1
- Most processed foods: <0.5:1

**Why this matters for Vincent's sodium-reduction goal:** The DASH and PURE trials both show that *raising potassium* matters at least as much as *lowering sodium* for blood-pressure outcomes — the body regulates BP via the Na/K balance, not sodium alone. A half avocado contributes 345 mg potassium with only 5 mg sodium, shifting the daily ratio meaningfully in the right direction. This is a much more efficient K-delivery vehicle than bananas (a half avocado has roughly the same K as a medium banana, with a similar calorie cost).

The 4700 mg/day K Adequate Intake is one of the most under-met nutrients in US diets (median intake ~2600 mg). Half an avocado covers ~7% of that gap in one move.

## Practical Serving Notes

- **Caloric density warning.** A whole medium Hass is ~227 kcal (SR Legacy) to ~280 kcal (FF entry). Visually it looks like a small fruit, but caloric-wise it's closer to ~3 slices of bread. Easy to under-count when eating intuitively. If tracking, default to **half = 115 kcal, whole = 230 kcal** as your mental anchor.
- **Fiber profile.** Total fiber 6.8 g/100 g is high, roughly **75% insoluble / 25% soluble** (literature pooled mean: insoluble ~2.63 g, soluble ~2.05 g per 100 g — note variance is high across samples). The insoluble fraction supports stool bulk; the soluble fraction (largely cellulose-pectin mix) is a fermentable substrate for gut microbiota.
- **Glycemic impact.** GI ~15 (very low — among the lowest of any fruit). The combination of high fat, high fiber, and very low free sugar (<0.5 g/100 g) means avocado essentially does not move blood glucose. Compatible with low-carb / time-restricted eating.
- **Ripeness window.** Hass goes from rock-hard to overripe in roughly 48 hours at room temp once it starts giving to gentle pressure. To slow ripening, refrigerate once it just starts to yield (fridge halts ethylene response within ~24 h).

## Storage: Preventing Browning

### Why avocado browns

Cut avocado flesh contains **polyphenol oxidase (PPO)**, a copper-containing enzyme, plus phenolic substrates (mainly catechins and chlorogenic-acid derivatives). When exposed to oxygen, PPO oxidizes these phenols into quinones, which then polymerize into brown melanin pigments. The reaction needs three things simultaneously: **enzyme + substrate + O₂**. Remove or block any one and browning stops.

Avocado PPO is famously hardy — it's one of the hardest plant PPOs to inactivate (Kahn 1975, *J Sci Food Agric*; comprehensive review: Weemaes et al., reviewed in *Compr Rev Food Sci Food Saf* 2016, PMID 27172067). Unlike apples or potatoes, you cannot meaningfully blanch a fresh avocado without ruining its texture, so the practical strategy is **block O₂ + lower pH**.

### Anti-browning tactics, ranked by effectiveness

| Rank | Method | Mechanism | How long stays green | Notes |
|---:|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Lemon/lime juice + airtight container** | Citric acid drops surface pH below 4 (PPO optimum is ~6); ascorbic acid reduces quinones back before they polymerize | **24–48 h** reliably, up to 3 days if heavily coated | Brush/squeeze juice over the *entire* exposed surface, not just a corner. This is the workhorse method; works because it attacks both the enzyme and the reaction product. |
| **2** | **Plastic wrap pressed flat against the cut surface** (or beeswax wrap) | Pure O₂ barrier — no enzyme inactivation, just exclusion | **~24 h**, sometimes longer if seal is perfect | Press out *all* air pockets. Combine with #1 for ~48 h. The California Avocado Commission's official recommendation for guacamole. |
| **3** | **Olive oil thin coat on cut surface** | Oxygen barrier (oil is largely impermeable to O₂); also adds flavor compatibility | **~24 h** | Same mechanism as plastic wrap but edible. Best when you'll eat the half cold the next day with bread or toast. |
| **4** | **Onion in the same airtight container** (cut side up, away from onion) | Sulfur compounds (allyl propyl disulfide, etc.) from Allium volatilize and inhibit PPO non-competitively. Bustos 2015 (LWT) showed Allium extracts preserved avocado paste >30 days at 4°C | **2–3 days**, sometimes up to a week with good seal | Surprisingly effective and well-supported in the food-science literature. Trade-off: avocado picks up a faint onion smell. Best if you'll use it in savory contexts. Combine with lemon for the strongest practical method. |
| **5** | **Vacuum sealing (handheld pump or vacuum bag)** | Removes O₂ from the headspace | **3–5 days** | Needs equipment. The most effective non-chemical method but most equipment-dependent. Combine with lemon for ~5–7 days. |
| **❌** | **Submerge in water in the fridge** | Excludes O₂ via water layer | Visually keeps it green 2–4 days | **DO NOT USE.** The FDA explicitly warns against this hack. FDA studies found *Listeria monocytogenes* on ~18% of sampled avocado skins; in refrigerated water immersion, *Listeria* can internalize into the avocado pulp within 15 days, and surface disinfecting won't remove it. The risk is real and the alternatives above all work. |
| **❌** | **Leave the pit in** | Internet myth | Negligible | The pit only protects the small area of flesh it physically contacts. The rest still browns. Don't waste mental energy on this. |

### Optimal fridge storage method for a half-used avocado

**Recommended Vincent-friendly protocol** (combines top three methods, no equipment, ~2 days reliable):

1. **Leave the pit in** the unused half (it doesn't *prevent* browning, but it does occupy surface area and slow water loss at the seed-cavity zone).
2. **Brush the cut surface** with fresh lemon or lime juice — coat the entire exposed flesh, not just the center. About 1/4 lemon's worth.
3. **Press a piece of plastic wrap directly onto the flesh**, pushing out air pockets. Wrap the rest of the half over the skin side.
4. **Place in a small airtight container** (the smaller the headspace, the better). Optionally toss in 1/4 of a yellow onion in a corner.
5. Refrigerate at 4°C.
6. Eat within 24–48 hours. If you slice and the top millimeter is brown, scrape it off — the layer underneath is usually fine.

**For ultra-low-friction (Vincent-grade lazy mode):** Just lemon juice on the cut surface + screw-top glass container. ~24 h reliable. The single highest-ROI tactic is the lemon juice — if you only do one thing, do that.

## Agent Decision Rules (rubric-aligned)

**When the agent should proactively suggest avocado:**
1. Daily Na projected ≥ 80% of 2300 mg UL → suggest ½ avocado at next meal (cheap K, near-zero Na).
2. Daily Na:K ratio projected > 1.0 → ½ avocado moves the ratio meaningfully without adding Na.
3. Daily K projected < 50% of 4700 mg AI by evening → suggest pairing with the next protein meal.
4. Vincent reports "fat" target under-met on a recomp strength day → MUFA-rich, fits the recomp profile.

**When NOT to suggest:**
- Vincent already over daily fat target by > 10 g.
- Vincent flagged caloric ceiling for the day and ≥ 200 kcal of headroom is needed for protein.
- Pre-climb meal (high fat slows gastric emptying — see `targets.md` 训练-进食时序: 攀岩前不吃高脂).

**Pairing rules (lipid-soluble vitamin synergy):**
- Avocado + tomato/salsa → boosts lycopene absorption (Unlu 2005, 2.6-4.4×).
- Avocado + leafy greens / carrots / sweet potato → boosts α/β-carotene absorption (~7-15×).
- Tag the daily-log row with `pairing:carotenoid-boost` if either co-occurs.
