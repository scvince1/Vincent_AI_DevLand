# NFLS Summer Program Recruitment & Planning Pattern-Break
**Time:** Dec 2020 - Aug 2024 (Vincent's Sr. PM tenure); reform implemented across multiple program cycles within this window
**Location:** NFLS (Nanjing Foreign Language School) — summer-program operations
**Status:** Concluded with Vincent's departure; pattern held during his tenure

## Real Facts
At NFLS, Vincent was Sr. PM responsible for summer-intensive programs for high-school students: Model UN, immersive theater, workshops, topical camps, and "baby internship" experiential programs. The industry-standard cadence was:

- **December (prior year):** Begin instructor recruitment.
- **February:** Lock teaching teams.
- **April - May:** Course development (content, lesson plans, logistics) compressed into roughly two months.
- **Summer:** Deliver the programs.

Results of that cadence: instructor burnout high by mid-program, student retention roughly 3-4 out of every 20 enrolled (i.e., heavy churn), and constant fire-fighting during delivery.

Vincent's pattern break: start the pipeline earlier, but softer.

- **October (roughly 14 weeks before industry norm):** Open casual networking with prospective instructors. Not formal recruitment. Coffee, conversations, "what would you want to teach if you had time." This surfaced motivated instructors plus let Vincent informally filter.
- **Mid-January:** Two teaching teams locked — weeks ahead of the industry February lock.
- **Mid-March:** Course development begins, earlier than the April-May crunch.

Actual schedule advance averaged 2-6 weeks depending on which program and which instructors ran point each year. The interview framing uses "2 weeks" — deliberately conservative.

Results: student retention improved from 3-4/20 to 6-8/20. Instructor churn reduced roughly 25%.

## Key Insights Extracted
- Chronic crunches almost always have an upstream fix. The crunch is a symptom of poor load distribution, not a symptom of insufficient summer capacity.
- Shift effort from high-stress weeks to low-stress weeks. The same work done in October takes half the emotional toll as the same work done in May.
- "Casual networking" phase is free optionality: no cost if nothing comes of it, high value if it surfaces a good instructor who wouldn't have entered the formal pipeline.
- Lock the early wins publicly (mid-January team locks), which sets expectations for peers that this is the new baseline.

## Follow-up Questions (if probed)
1. "How did you convince leadership to let you start in October?" — Low cost, no formal hire commitment; framed as informal networking, not recruitment.
2. "Did other PMs adopt the model?" — Some did; adoption was gradual and uneven.
3. "How did you measure retention improvement?" — Enrollment-to-completion ratios across program cycles.
4. "Biggest resistance?" — Instructors used to the industry cadence; some found the October outreach weird at first.
5. "What would you do with more resources?" — Extend the same logic further upstream: alumni networks, instructor pipeline from prior year's participants.

## Sensitivities / Flags
- Numbers are presented conservatively ("2 weeks" improvement). Actual 2-6 weeks across program-years. Safe to hold.
- Retention numbers (3-4/20 → 6-8/20) are directionally accurate; if pressed for exact year-by-year: "the improvement compounded across program cycles."
- No meaningful exposure. This is one of the cleanest stories.
