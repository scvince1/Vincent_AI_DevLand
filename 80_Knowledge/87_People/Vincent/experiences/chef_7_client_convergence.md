# Private Chef - 7-Client Convergence Week
**Time:** During the Macalester private-chef period, 2021-2023 (specific week unmarked)
**Location:** Minnesota (Twin Cities / Macalester area)
**Status:** Concluded (one specific week within the broader chef chapter)

## Real Facts
During the private-chef years (see `private_chef_minnesota.md`), the normal client load was 3-4 recurring clients, deliberately spaced across the week so delivery days did not collide. One week, 7 clients' orders converged on the same delivery day — a combination of calendar quirks, event-driven catering requests, and one or two clients moving their standing day.

Volume: roughly 200 meals across different menus. Each client had their own diet requirements, texture preferences, and delivery windows.

Vincent's pivot from craft-mode to batch-mode:
- **French-style pan-sears** for anything protein that could be prepped batch-wise and finished fast.
- **Fryer** for components where finish-crispness tolerated a short holding window.
- **Pressure cooker** for stews / braises that could be built once and portioned out.
- **Oven** running in parallel for roasts and bakes where the slot window was long enough.
- **Shelf-ingredient garnishes** instead of a la minute garnishing — accept a small quality loss for throughput.
- **Food-science considerations for freeze/reheat quality** — recipes chosen and adjusted for which dishes would hold best over the delivery window.

Every order went out. No missed delivery across the full private-chef career.

## Key Insights Extracted
- Don't chase perfection under a fixed deadline. Prioritize and deliver.
- Ship first, polish later. A delivered "good enough" beats an undelivered "perfection."
- Under load, change the method, not the menu promise. Swap in batch-friendly techniques invisible to the client.
- Food science (freeze / reheat / holding behavior) is the quiet operations lever behind every convincing delivery promise.

## Follow-up Questions (if probed)
1. "How did you prioritize which client got which slot?" — Delivery-window strictness first (some clients had hard windows), then complexity of reheat-quality risk.
2. "What was the quality tradeoff?" — Small. Shelf garnishes instead of fresh-cut; batch proteins instead of a la minute. Clients didn't notice.
3. "Did you ever miss a delivery in the broader business?" — Never, across the full chef career.
4. "How did you recover from that week?" — Re-spaced the schedule afterwards and added a hard cap on same-day convergence.
5. "What technique surprised you as a batch tool?" — Pressure cooker; wildly underrated for quality-per-throughput on braises.
