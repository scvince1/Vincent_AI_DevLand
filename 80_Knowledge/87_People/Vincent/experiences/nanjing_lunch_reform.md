# Off-Campus Lunch Reform (十一学校)
**Time:** Real event 2014-2015 (Vincent ages 16-17, student at the time). Interview version reframes to ~2022 at NFLS.
**Location:** Real: 十一学校 (Beijing No. 11 School / RDFZ), Haidian, Beijing. Interview version: NFLS (Nanjing Foreign Language School), Nanjing.
**Status:** Concluded; policy stuck at 十一学校 post-reform.

## Real Facts
Around 2014-2015 at 十一学校 (Vincent attended 2012-2018), the central cafeteria was overloaded serving roughly 5,000 people. Leadership wanted to allow students to leave campus for lunch at nearby restaurants to relieve pressure. Resistance came from every stakeholder group and for different reasons:

- **Teachers:** Worried about liability if a student was hurt, got sick, or skipped class during the extended window.
- **Parents:** Split. Some welcomed the flexibility; others feared food safety, traffic risk, or that their child would use the time irresponsibly.
- **Leadership:** Wanted the reform but needed political cover — couldn't unilaterally impose.
- **Nearby businesses / mall / hospital / local authorities:** Needed to understand what a sudden influx of minors meant for their operations and liability.

Vincent helped lead outreach. Key moves:
1. Expanded the student affairs office's working scope to include off-campus policy coordination.
2. Built an escalation protocol: nearby hospital on-call contact, mall security and restaurant managers briefed, identified opinion-leader parents to broker wider parent community, direct line to local business owners for issue reporting.
3. Addressed each stakeholder on their own terms: teachers got a liability shield via the protocol; parents got a channel plus peer-parent endorsements; businesses got predictable behavior expectations.

Reform passed. Off-campus lunch became a standing policy.

## Key Insights Extracted
- Every group says no for a different reason. One unified argument convinces nobody.
- Map the resistance per-stakeholder, then address each in the language that group actually uses.
- Infrastructure (hospital, mall, local businesses) already exists — route risk through it rather than build new safety apparatus from scratch.
- Opinion-leader parents unlock broader parent cohorts; identifying them is more efficient than mass communication.
