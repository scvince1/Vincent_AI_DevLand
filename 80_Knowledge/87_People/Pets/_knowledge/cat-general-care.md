# Cat General Care & Household Toxin Overview

*Created: 2026-04-14 | Sources: AAFP, AAHA, WSAVA, ASPCA APCC, Cornell Feline Health Center, American Heartworm Society, peer-reviewed literature*

---

## 1. Vaccination — Core vs. Non-Core

### 1.1 Classification Table (2020 AAHA/AAFP Guidelines)

| Vaccine | Classification | Kittens | Adults | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **FVRCP** (FHV-1 + FCV + FPV) | Core | Every 3-4 wks until 16-20 wks; booster at 6 mo | Then every 3 years | See §1.2 for 2020 booster change |
| **Rabies** | Core | Single dose; follow label + local law | 3-year interval (3-yr labeled product) | Protection within 28 days of first dose |
| **FeLV** | Core for <1 yr; Non-core for low-risk adults | 2 doses, 3-4 wks apart | Annual for high-risk; skip for confirmed low-risk | Test for FeLV antigen before vaccinating |
| **FIV** | Not available in North America | — | — | Prior vaccination (pre-2015) can cause false-positive antibody tests for 7+ years; newer tests can differentiate |
| *Chlamydia felis* | Non-core | Risk-based only | Risk-based | Multi-cat households, catteries |
| *Bordetella bronchiseptica* | Non-core | Risk-based only | Risk-based | Cats with dogs, boarding |
| **FIP** | Not recommended | — | — | Insufficient field evidence of protection |

### 1.2 Key 2020 AAHA/AAFP Update Points

1. **6-month FVRCP booster** — replaces the prior 1-year booster; targets the window when maternally-derived antibodies (MDA) may blunt the final kitten dose, leaving a gap in immunity.
2. **FeLV reclassified by age** — previously universal core; now core only for kittens (<1 yr) due to reduced exposure risk in low-risk adult indoor cats.
3. **Injection site protocols** — distal limb vaccination preferred to maximize surgical margins if feline injection-site sarcoma (FISS) develops. Document site at every visit.
4. **Non-adjuvanted formulations** — may carry lower FISS risk; evidence insufficient to mandate, but preferred where options exist.
5. **Individualized risk-benefit reassessment** — recommended annually based on lifestyle, health status, and exposure changes.

### 1.3 Adult Booster Frequency Summary

- **FVRCP (parenteral):** Every 3 years after kitten series is complete.
- **FVRCP (intranasal):** Annual revaccination recommended.
- **Rabies:** Per label and local ordinance; 3-year interval with 3-year labeled products where permitted.
- **FeLV (high-risk adults):** Annually.

## 2. Parasite Prevention — Miami/Doral Subtropical Context

### 2.1 Heartworm

**Why indoor cats are not exempt:** Mosquitoes — the obligate vector — enter homes through windows, doors, and gaps. Indoor cats in South Florida face year-round exposure.

**Feline-specific biology:**
- Cats are atypical hosts; adult worm burden typically 1-6 (vs. 30+ in dogs).
- Even immature worms cause **HARD** (Heartworm Associated Respiratory Disease): coughing, dyspnea, vomiting — often misdiagnosed as asthma or allergic bronchitis.
- No approved adulticide treatment exists for cats; prevention is the only viable strategy.

**Testing:** Dual-test protocol recommended — **antigen + antibody** test (antibody test detects larval exposure before adult worms develop). Baseline before starting prevention; annually thereafter.

**Prevention:** Year-round, monthly topical or oral preventive; start no later than 8 weeks of age. Consult veterinarian for product selection (many feline heartworm preventives also cover intestinal parasites).

### 2.2 Fleas

**Miami risk:** Year-round high risk — subtropical climate supports *Ctenocephalides felis* populations continuously. Indoor cats can acquire fleas from owner clothing, shoes, or other pets.

- Fleas transmit *Dipylidium caninum* (tapeworm) and *Bartonella henselae* (cat scratch disease, zoonotic).
- Monthly topical or oral prevention recommended year-round.
- Products containing imidacloprid, selamectin, or fluralaner (bimonthly) are AVMA-recognized options; confirm cat-safe formulation — **never use dog-labeled permethrin products on cats** (acutely toxic, potentially fatal).

### 2.3 Ticks

**Miami risk:** Moderate; *Amblyomma americanum* and *Rhipicephalus sanguineus* are endemic in Florida. Indoor-only cats have low but non-zero exposure (via clothing, other pets).

- Ticks transmit *Cytauxzoon felis* (potentially fatal) and *Rickettsia* spp.
- Risk-based prevention; combination products covering fleas + ticks available for cats. Confirm cat-safe formulations.

### 2.4 Intestinal Parasites

- Fecal examination: strongly recommended for kittens (3-4x in first year); annually for adult indoor cats.
- *Toxocara cati* is zoonotic — relevant especially in households with children.
- Monthly broad-spectrum prevention often bundled with heartworm products.

## 3. Annual Wellness Exam — Age-Stratified Diagnostic Tiers

Per 2021 AAHA/AAFP Feline Life Stage Guidelines:

| Test | <7 yrs (Young Adult) | 7-10 yrs (Mature Adult) | >10 yrs (Senior/Geriatric) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physical exam + weight + BCS + MCS | Annually | Annually | Every 6 months (or more) |
| CBC | Baseline; as needed | Every 1-2 yrs (recommended) | Every visit (strongly recommended) |
| Serum biochemistry panel | Baseline; as needed | Every 1-2 yrs | Every visit |
| Urinalysis | Baseline; as needed | Every 1-2 yrs | Every visit |
| Total T4 (thyroid) | Consider at mature end | Recommended | Strongly recommended |
| SDMA / renal indices | Consider | Recommended | Strongly recommended |
| Blood pressure | Baseline; as needed | Recommended | Every visit |
| Fecal exam | Strongly recommended (kittens) | Annually | Annually |

**Exam frequency:** Minimum annually for healthy adults; **every 6 months** for cats >10 years or those with chronic disease.

*Rationale: Cat lifespans are ~5x shorter than humans; annual veterinary exams are equivalent to humans seeing a doctor every 5 years — underscoring why senior frequency must increase.*

## 4. Weight & BCS Monitoring

**WSAVA 9-point BCS scale:** Ideal BCS = 4-5/9. Risk of obesity-associated disease increases above BCS 6/9. Approximately 30%+ of pet cats in Western populations are overweight or obese.

**Muscle Condition Score (MCS):** Assess separately — senior cats can be normal weight but sarcopenic; MCS decline precedes weight loss in CKD and hyperthyroidism.

**Recommended tracking cadence:**

| Life stage | Weight | BCS + MCS |
|---|---|---|
| Kitten | Every visit | Every visit |
| Young adult (1-6 yrs) | Annually | Annually |
| Mature adult (7-10 yrs) | Every 6-12 months | Every 6-12 months |
| Senior (>10 yrs) | Every visit (every 6 mo min) | Every visit |
| Any active medical condition | Per condition protocol | Per condition protocol |

**Home monitoring:** Digital kitchen scale (accurate to 10 g); record weekly for cats on weight-management plans. A 0.5 kg loss over 6 months in a senior cat warrants workup even if weight remains in "normal" range — trending matters more than single data points.

## 5. Dental Health

### 5.1 Epidemiology

50-90% of cats >4 years have some form of dental disease (Cornell Feline). Tooth resorption affects 30-70% of cats and is the leading cause of tooth loss.

### 5.2 COHAT — Indications

**Comprehensive Oral Health Assessment and Treatment (COHAT)** is the standard professional procedure; requires general anesthesia for complete evaluation.

Indications include:
- Visible tartar/calculus accumulation
- Persistent halitosis
- Gingivitis (red, inflamed gum line)
- Reluctance to eat, pawing at mouth, drooling, behavioral change suggesting oral pain
- Suspected tooth resorption (pinkish lesion at gum-tooth junction)
- Routine: baseline by age 2-3; interval thereafter per veterinary assessment (commonly annually or every 1-2 years for adults)

COHAT includes: full-mouth dental radiographs, periodontal probing, scaling, polishing, extractions as needed, and written home care plan.

### 5.3 Home Dental Care

- **Brushing:** Gold standard. Use feline-specific enzymatic toothpaste (CET or VOHC-approved product). Bristles at 45° angle to gum-tooth junction; focus on outer surfaces. Daily is ideal; 3x/week minimum for clinical benefit. **Never use human toothpaste** (fluoride, xylitol — both toxic to cats).
- **Gradual introduction:** 4-week acclimation protocol — finger + flavor → gauze → soft brush → standard brush.
- **Adjuncts (VOHC-accepted only):** Dental diets, water additives, dental chews labeled with VOHC Seal of Acceptance.

## 6. Senior Cat (>10 yrs) Special Considerations

### 6.1 The "Big Four" Conditions

| Condition | Prevalence | Screening Tool | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CKD** | ~30-35% of cats >10 yrs | Creatinine + SDMA + USG + UPCR + BP | Every 3-6 months once diagnosed (IRIS staging) |
| **Hyperthyroidism** | ~10% of cats >10 yrs | Total T4 ± free T4/TSH | Annually; more frequent if borderline or treated |
| **Systemic Hypertension** | Secondary to CKD/hyperthyroidism | Indirect Doppler or oscillometric BP | Every visit for at-risk cats; q6mo for IRIS Stage 2+ |
| **DJD (Degenerative Joint Disease)** | Up to 74% of cats ≥12 yrs | History (jumping, grooming, elimination changes) + 6-question screen + radiographs | Annually; NSAIDs contraindicated with concurrent CKD |

### 6.2 CKD Monitoring Detail

- IRIS staging based on fasting serum creatinine (repeated; must be stable, hydrated).
- **SDMA >14 µg/dL** suggests renal dysfunction even with normal creatinine.
- **Urine specific gravity <1.035** warrants reassessment.
- Substage by blood pressure (hypertensive substage: SBP ≥160 mmHg) and UPCR (proteinuria substage: UPCR ≥0.4).
- Once stable: monitoring every 3-6 months; more frequently during treatment adjustment.

### 6.3 Hyperthyroidism Note

T4 may be suppressed into high-normal range in cats with concurrent CKD, cancer, or systemic illness — masking hyperthyroidism. Track **T4 trends** over serial visits rather than relying on single values. Free T4 + TSH indicated when clinical suspicion is high but total T4 is borderline.

### 6.4 DJD Management

DJD and CKD co-occur in ~68% of affected senior cats — NSAID use is controversial in this population. Environmental modifications (ramps, lower litter box entry, elevated food bowl) are first-line; consult veterinarian before initiating any analgesic in cats with renal compromise.

## 7. Household Toxin Overview

*Macro-level reference. Detailed AKI/nephrotoxin and pharmaceutical toxin protocols are maintained in separate KB files. This section cross-references them without duplication.*

### 7.1 Food Toxins

| Toxin | Mechanism | Clinical Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Allium spp. (onion, garlic, chives, leeks — raw, cooked, powdered) | Heinz body hemolytic anemia; RBC oxidative damage | Severe; dose-dependent; garlic more potent than onion per weight |
| Chocolate / caffeine / theobromine | CNS stimulation, cardiac arrhythmia, diuresis | Moderate-severe; dark chocolate > milk chocolate |
| Alcohol (ethanol) | CNS depression, hypoglycemia, acidosis | Severe even in small quantities |
| Grapes / raisins | Mechanism unknown; idiosyncratic nephrotoxin | Severe; AKI reported — **see AKI KB** |
| Xylitol (sugar-free products, some peanut butters, toothpaste) | Hepatotoxicity in cats (less hypoglycemic than in dogs, but still dangerous) | Moderate-severe |
| Raw fish (thiaminase) | Thiamine (B1) deficiency with chronic feeding | Neurological signs; cumulative |

### 7.2 Chemical / Household Toxins

| Toxin | Route of Exposure | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Ethylene glycol** (antifreeze, brake fluid) | Ingestion; sweet taste | Acute oxalate nephropathy — **see AKI KB**; rapidly fatal without treatment within hours |
| **Bleach / chlorine** (undiluted) | Ingestion, skin/mucous membrane | Caustic burns, esophageal damage; diluted/dried solutions lower risk |
| **Essential oils** (tea tree, eucalyptus, citrus, pennyroyal, cinnamon, clove, pine) | Dermal, inhalation, ingestion | Cats lack hepatic glucuronyl transferase → impaired metabolism; hepatotoxicity, CNS depression, aspiration pneumonia risk |
| **Permethrin / pyrethroid insecticides** (dog spot-ons, yard sprays) | Dermal absorption | Tremors, seizures, hyperthermia — **acutely life-threatening in cats**; never use dog-labeled products on cats |
| **Laundry pods / fabric softener / dryer sheets** | Ingestion | Cationic detergents → severe chemical burns to mouth, esophagus, stomach; foreign body risk |
| **Benzalkonium chloride** (disinfectant sprays, sanitizers) | Dermal, ingestion (grooming) | Oral ulceration, profuse salivation, respiratory distress |
| **Rodenticides** (anticoagulant, bromethalin, zinc phosphide) | Ingestion (primary or relay) | Anticoagulant: coagulopathy; bromethalin: cerebral edema; see specific toxin KB |

### 7.3 Plant Toxins

| Plant | Toxicity Mechanism | Clinical Severity |
|---|---|---|
| **True lilies** — Lilium spp. (Easter, Tiger, Asian, Stargazer, Rubrum, Wood) and *Hemerocallis* spp. (daylilies) | Unknown nephrotoxin | **Fatal AKI within 36-72 hrs; even pollen ingestion is dangerous — see AKI KB** |
| **Sago palm** (*Cycas revoluta*) | Cycasin hepatotoxin | Severe hepatotoxicity; high mortality; seeds most concentrated |
| **Aloe vera** | Anthraquinone glycosides | GI: vomiting, diarrhea, lethargy; electrolyte disturbance |
| **Snake plant / Sansevieria** | Saponins | Mild-moderate GI upset |
| **Monstera / Swiss cheese plant** | Insoluble calcium oxalates | Oral irritation, swelling, hypersalivation, dysphagia |
| **Pothos / Devil's Ivy** (*Epipremnum aureum*) | Insoluble calcium oxalates | Oral/GI irritation — cross-reference Plant KB |
| **Philodendron spp.** | Insoluble calcium oxalates | Oral/GI irritation — cross-reference Plant KB |
| **Dieffenbachia / Dumbcane** | Insoluble calcium oxalates + proteolytic enzymes | Intense oral pain, swelling, potential airway compromise |

**Zero-tolerance rule for cat households:** True lilies (*Lilium* and *Hemerocallis* spp.) should be completely excluded — no exceptions. Even water from lily vases can cause lethal AKI.
